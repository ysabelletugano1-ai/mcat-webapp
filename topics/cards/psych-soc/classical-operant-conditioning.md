# Classical & Operant Conditioning
**AAMC Category:** 7C — Attitude and behavior change | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Classical conditioning (Pavlov) creates an association between a neutral stimulus and a reflex-triggering stimulus, eventually eliciting the reflex without the original trigger. Operant conditioning (Skinner) shapes voluntary behavior through consequences (reinforcers and punishers). Both are foundational learning theories heavily tested on MCAT Psych/Soc.

## High-Yield Facts
**Classical Conditioning:**
- **UCS** (unconditioned stimulus): naturally triggers response (food → salivation)
- **UCR** (unconditioned response): natural, unlearned reflex
- **CS** (conditioned stimulus): previously neutral; after pairing with UCS, triggers response alone
- **CR** (conditioned response): learned response to CS (same as UCR but often weaker)
- **Extinction**: CS presented without UCS → CR fades
- **Spontaneous recovery**: CR returns after rest period without reconditioning
- **Stimulus generalization**: CR occurs to stimuli similar to CS
- **Stimulus discrimination**: CR occurs only to specific CS, not similar stimuli

**Operant Conditioning:**
- **Positive reinforcement**: add pleasant stimulus → behavior increases (treat for good behavior)
- **Negative reinforcement**: remove unpleasant stimulus → behavior increases (seatbelt alarm stops when buckled)
- **Positive punishment**: add unpleasant stimulus → behavior decreases (scolding)
- **Negative punishment**: remove pleasant stimulus → behavior decreases (take away phone)
- **Continuous reinforcement**: fastest acquisition, fastest extinction
- **Partial reinforcement schedules**: slower acquisition, greater resistance to extinction
- **Variable ratio schedule**: most resistant to extinction (gambling)

## Mnemonic
**"Positive = add, Negative = remove; Reinforcement = increases behavior, Punishment = decreases"**
For schedules: **"Variable Ratio = Very Resistant"** (to extinction)

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: r/MCAT 520+ psych/soc guide*
Negative reinforcement is the most misunderstood concept on MCAT Psych/Soc. **Negative reinforcement INCREASES behavior** — it's still reinforcement. The "negative" refers to removing something bad (negative = taking away). Example: you take pain medication (behavior) because it removes pain (removes bad stimulus) → you take it again. The pain removal reinforces the behavior of taking medication.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Extinction in classical conditioning ≠ forgetting. Extinction means the CS-CR association is suppressed (not erased) — proven by spontaneous recovery, where the CR returns after a rest period without any reconditioning. The original learning persists at some level.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A child is bitten by a dog (UCS). Now the child cries (CR) whenever they see any dog (CS). Later, they stop crying at the sight of dogs after many dog encounters with no biting. Then after a vacation with no dogs, they cry again upon seeing a dog. What learning principles are illustrated?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Extinction followed by spontaneous recovery.** The repeated CS (dog) without UCS (bite) → CR (crying) diminished = extinction. The recovery of crying after the rest period (vacation) without any new conditioning = spontaneous recovery. This demonstrates that extinction suppresses, not erases, the conditioned association.
</details>

**Q2.** A researcher removes a rat's food pellet reward whenever it presses a lever, in order to stop lever-pressing. Which operant conditioning procedure is this?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Negative punishment.** A pleasant stimulus (food pellet) is REMOVED following the behavior (lever pressing) to DECREASE the behavior. Positive/negative = add/remove; punishment = decrease behavior. (Not to be confused with extinction, which involves withholding the reinforcer entirely — here the pellet is actively taken away.)
</details>

**Q3.** A slot machine pays out on a variable ratio schedule. Why is this schedule the most resistant to extinction?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Because the reinforcement is unpredictable — the gambler never knows when the next reward will come.** Variable ratio: reinforcement after an unpredictable number of responses. The unpredictability prevents the learner from detecting that reinforcement has stopped (compared to fixed schedules, where absence at the expected time signals extinction). This is why gambling behavior is so persistent even when losing.
</details>

**Q4.** A student studies harder after receiving praise from their professor. The praise is added after studying (behavior increases). What type of conditioning and what specific procedure applies?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Operant conditioning — positive reinforcement.** A pleasant stimulus (praise) is ADDED following the behavior (studying), causing the behavior to INCREASE. This is operant because studying is a voluntary behavior shaped by its consequences, and positive because a stimulus is being added.
</details>

**Q5.** In Little Albert's experiment, Watson conditioned fear of a white rat (CS) by pairing it with a loud noise (UCS). Albert later showed fear of white rabbits and fur coats, even without conditioning. What phenomenon does this illustrate?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Stimulus generalization.** Albert's conditioned fear response (CR) generalized to stimuli similar to the original CS (white rat) — white rabbit, fur coat, Santa Claus beard. The CR occurs to stimuli that share features with the CS, even without direct conditioning. (Note: Little Albert's experiment is also an example of classical conditioning applied to human emotion, demonstrating that phobias can be learned.)
</details>

# Psychological Disorders
**AAMC Category:** 7A — Individual influences on behavior | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Psychological disorders represent patterns of behavior, cognition, or emotion that cause significant distress or impair functioning. MCAT Psych/Soc tests knowledge of major disorders, their neurobiological underpinnings (neurotransmitters, brain structures), and their treatments. The biopsychosocial model — integrating biological, psychological, and social factors — provides the comprehensive framework the MCAT favors over the purely biomedical model.

## High-Yield Facts
**Schizophrenia:**
- **Positive symptoms** (things ADDED): hallucinations (auditory most common), delusions, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior
- **Negative symptoms** (things TAKEN AWAY): flat affect, alogia (poverty of speech), avolition (lack of motivation), anhedonia
- **Dopamine hypothesis**: excess dopamine activity (especially in mesolimbic pathway) underlies positive symptoms
- **Treatment**: antipsychotics (typical = block D2 receptors broadly; atypical = block D2 + serotonin receptors; better for negative symptoms)

**Depression (Major Depressive Disorder):**
- Decreased serotonin, norepinephrine (NE), and dopamine
- Symptoms: depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, changes in sleep/appetite, guilt, poor concentration, suicidal ideation (2+ weeks, most of the day)
- **Treatment**: SSRIs (first-line), SNRIs, TCAs, MAOIs; psychotherapy (CBT)

**Anxiety Disorders:**
- Mechanism: hyperactivation of amygdala + HPA axis → sympathetic nervous system activation (fight-or-flight)
- **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)**: excessive worry about multiple domains, 6+ months
- **Panic disorder**: recurrent unexpected panic attacks + worry about future attacks
- **Specific phobia**: intense fear of specific object/situation
- **Social anxiety disorder**: fear of social scrutiny/humiliation
- **Treatment**: SSRIs/SNRIs (first-line), benzodiazepines (short-term), CBT/exposure therapy

**OCD and PTSD (moved out of Anxiety in DSM-5):**
- **OCD**: obsessions (intrusive thoughts) + compulsions (repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety); serotonin dysregulation; treated with SSRIs + ERP (exposure and response prevention)
- **PTSD**: following trauma; re-experiencing (flashbacks), avoidance, negative cognition/mood, hyperarousal; treated with trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, SSRIs

**Bipolar Disorder:**
- Manic episodes (elevated/irritable mood, decreased sleep, grandiosity, impulsivity, increased goal-directed activity) + depressive episodes
- Bipolar I: full manic episodes; Bipolar II: hypomania + depression
- Treatment: **lithium** (mood stabilizer, first-line), anticonvulsants (valproate), atypical antipsychotics

**ADHD:**
- Dopamine and norepinephrine dysregulation in prefrontal cortex
- Inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity; onset before age 12
- Treatment: stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamines = increase DA/NE); non-stimulants (atomoxetine = SNRI)

**Biomedical vs. Biopsychosocial Model:**
- **Biomedical model**: disease = biological malfunction; ignores psychological/social factors
- **Biopsychosocial model** (Engel): disease = interaction of biological (genetics, neurotransmitters), psychological (thoughts, emotions, behavior), and social (SES, relationships, culture) factors → more comprehensive and MCAT-preferred

## Mnemonic
**Schizophrenia symptoms:** "Positive = Plus (hallucinations ADDED). Negative = Minus (affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia REMOVED)."
**Neurotransmitters:** "Serotonin = Sad (depression). Dopamine = Delusional (schizophrenia). NE = Nervous (anxiety)."
**Bipolar treatment:** "Lithium = Leveling the highs and lows."

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: r/MCAT 520+ guides*
Schizophrenia positive symptoms = things ADDED (hallucinations, delusions). Negative symptoms = things TAKEN AWAY (flat affect, poverty of speech). Antipsychotics block dopamine D2 receptors = treat positive symptoms more than negative symptoms. This is why negative symptoms are harder to treat — they are not simply from dopamine excess. On MCAT passages: if a new drug blocks D2 receptors, it will improve hallucinations and delusions more than flat affect or avolition.

## Common Trap
⚠️ OCD and PTSD were classified as Anxiety Disorders in DSM-IV but are now in their own separate categories in DSM-5 (OCD in "Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders"; PTSD in "Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders"). **On MCAT, focus on the clinical characteristics, neurobiology, and treatment of these disorders — not on DSM category reorganization.** MCAT passages will test whether you can identify OCD from a clinical description (obsessions + compulsions) and distinguish it from GAD (worry without compulsions), not whether you remember which DSM chapter it belongs to.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A patient is brought to the emergency department because they believe the government has implanted a tracking device in their brain and has been hearing voices telling them to take action. They also show flat affect and speak very little. Classify these symptoms as positive or negative, and name the disorder.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Schizophrenia.** Positive symptoms (things added): delusions (belief about government tracking device) and auditory hallucinations (voices). Negative symptoms (things taken away): flat affect (blunted emotional expression) and alogia (poverty of speech). This is the classic MCAT schizophrenia presentation. The dopamine hypothesis explains the positive symptoms (excess dopamine activity); negative symptoms are harder to treat because they are not simply from dopamine excess.
</details>

**Q2.** Match each neurotransmitter imbalance to its associated disorder: (A) decreased serotonin and NE, (B) excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathway, (C) dopamine/NE dysregulation in prefrontal cortex.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**(A) Decreased serotonin and NE → Major Depressive Disorder.** (B) Excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathway → Schizophrenia (positive symptoms). (C) Dopamine/NE dysregulation in prefrontal cortex → ADHD. These are the three most heavily tested neurotransmitter-disorder associations on MCAT Psych/Soc. Note: anxiety disorders involve serotonin dysregulation too (hence SSRIs as first-line), but the primary mechanism is amygdala/HPA hyperactivation.
</details>

**Q3.** A 28-year-old patient with generalized anxiety disorder and panic disorder would most likely be prescribed which class of medication as first-line treatment, and why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors)** are first-line for both GAD and panic disorder. SSRIs increase serotonin availability by blocking reuptake at the synapse, which reduces amygdala hyperactivation and HPA axis dysregulation over time (weeks). Benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam) may be used short-term for acute anxiety but are not first-line long-term due to dependence risk. CBT (particularly exposure therapy) is also first-line for anxiety disorders, often combined with medication.
</details>

**Q4.** A physician evaluating a patient with chronic migraines considers not just the neurological pathology but also the patient's high-stress job, history of childhood trauma, poor social support, and low-income neighborhood. What model of health is the physician applying, and how does it differ from the biomedical model?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Biopsychosocial model** (Engel, 1977). The physician is integrating biological (neurological pathology), psychological (history of trauma, stress response), and social (job stress, social support, SES/neighborhood) factors. The **biomedical model** would focus exclusively on the biological/neurological mechanism and treat only the physiological pathology, ignoring psychological and social contributors. The biopsychosocial model is more comprehensive and is the framework explicitly favored by AAMC on the MCAT.
</details>

**Q5.** Distinguish the anxiety features of OCD from those of Generalized Anxiety Disorder. A patient says, "I can't stop worrying that something bad will happen to my family, and I check the door locks 30 times every night even though I know it's irrational." Which disorder does this most suggest, and why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**OCD.** The key diagnostic feature distinguishing OCD from GAD is the presence of **compulsions** (repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety from obsessions). GAD involves excessive, difficult-to-control worry across multiple domains — without compulsions. OCD involves: (1) **obsessions** (intrusive, unwanted, anxiety-producing thoughts — here: fear of harm to family) + (2) **compulsions** (repetitive behaviors to neutralize the anxiety — here: checking locks 30 times). The patient recognizes the checking is excessive (ego-dystonic), which is characteristic of OCD (vs. delusional disorder, where the belief is ego-syntonic).
</details>

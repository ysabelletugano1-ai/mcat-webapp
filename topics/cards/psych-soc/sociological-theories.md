# Sociological Theories
**AAMC Category:** 9A — Understanding social structure | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Sociological theories provide frameworks for understanding how society is organized and how social forces shape individual behavior. MCAT tests three major paradigms — Functionalism, Conflict Theory, and Symbolic Interactionism — plus Goffman's dramaturgical model and the concept of social constructionism. Each theory explains the same social institutions through a different lens, and MCAT passages require identifying which framework is being applied.

## High-Yield Facts
**Functionalism (Durkheim, Parsons, Merton):**
- Society = a system of interdependent parts; each element serves a function that maintains social stability
- **Manifest functions**: intended, recognized consequences of a social institution (school → educate)
- **Latent functions**: unintended, unrecognized consequences (school → provide childcare, build peer networks)
- **Dysfunction**: elements that disrupt social stability
- **Anomie** (Durkheim): normlessness; breakdown of social norms during rapid social change → social dysfunction, increased suicide rates
- Focus: social order, consensus, stability

**Conflict Theory (Marx, Weber, Collins):**
- Society = ongoing struggle between groups for power and resources
- Inequality is not natural or functional — it is maintained by those in power to serve their own interests
- **Marx**: class conflict between bourgeoisie (owners of production) and proletariat (workers)
- **Weber**: stratification based on three dimensions — class (economic), status (prestige), party (political power)
- Focus: power, inequality, social change, exploitation

**Symbolic Interactionism (Mead, Blumer, Goffman):**
- Focus on **micro-level** interactions — how individuals create meaning through symbols, language, and face-to-face interaction
- Reality is socially constructed through shared meanings
- **Goffman's Dramaturgical Model**: social life as theatrical performance
  - **Front stage**: behavior when aware of being observed; impression management
  - **Back stage**: behavior when away from audience; more authentic self
  - **Impression management**: active effort to control how others perceive you
- **Social Constructionism**: concepts (race, gender, mental illness) are created and given meaning by society, not purely natural categories

**Social Institutions:**
- Family, education, religion, economy, government — all can be analyzed through any of the three frameworks
- Functionalism: how does each institution maintain stability?
- Conflict theory: who benefits from this institution and who is disadvantaged?
- Symbolic Interactionism: what meanings do people attach to this institution?

**McDonaldization (Ritzer):** rationalization of society — efficiency, calculability, predictability, control applied to all social domains

**"Durkheim = Drum of harmony. Marx = Megaphone of conflict. Mead/Goffman = Mirror of meaning."**

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
When the MCAT asks "which theory focuses on how power shapes inequality?" → Conflict theory. "Which focuses on symbols and micro-level meaning?" → Symbolic Interactionism. "Which focuses on how institutions maintain social stability?" → Functionalism. These three questions cover 90% of MCAT sociological theory questions. For Goffman: any passage about impression management, identity performance, or front/back stage behavior → Symbolic Interactionism and dramaturgical model.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Functionalism and Conflict theory can BOTH analyze the same institution (e.g., education/school) and reach opposite conclusions. Functionalism: education serves society by transmitting knowledge and creating shared values (maintains order). Conflict theory: education reproduces inequality — wealthy families have advantages (better schools, social capital) that perpetuate class hierarchies. The theory chosen changes the entire interpretation. MCAT passages will describe data and ask which theory best explains it — look for whether the passage emphasizes stability/function or power/inequality.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A sociologist argues that religion serves to reinforce social cohesion by providing shared rituals and values that bind community members together. Which sociological perspective does this represent, and what specific concept is being invoked?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Functionalism**, invoking the concept of **manifest function** (or social cohesion/solidarity, as Durkheim described). Functionalism analyzes institutions by the role they play in maintaining social stability — religion here is serving the function of creating shared norms and solidarity. If this were described as an unintended benefit of religion, it would specifically be a **latent function**. Conflict theorists would instead ask who holds power within religious institutions and who is marginalized.
</details>

**Q2.** Following a major economic recession, sociologist Emile Durkheim would predict an increase in which social phenomenon, and why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Anomie and associated dysfunctions (including elevated suicide rates).** Rapid social disruption (like economic collapse) breaks down established social norms and the regulatory framework that gives individuals purpose and direction. Durkheim coined the term anomie to describe this normlessness. In his study of suicide, he found that anomic suicide (from normlessness/social disruption) increased during periods of rapid economic change — both downturns AND booms — because both destabilize the social order that individuals depend on for meaning.
</details>

**Q3.** A medical student behaves very professionally and formally during clinical rounds (white coat, measured speech, deferential to attendings). After rounds, in the break room with fellow students, they complain, joke, and speak casually. According to Goffman, what does each behavior represent?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Front stage vs. back stage behavior (dramaturgical model).** During rounds = **front stage**: the student is performing for an audience (attendings, patients), engaging in impression management to project a specific identity (competent, professional). In the break room = **back stage**: away from the primary audience, the student drops the performance and behaves more authentically. Goffman argued all social life involves this theatrical quality — we are always managing impressions for different audiences.
</details>

**Q4.** A functionalist sociologist notes that public schools transmit academic knowledge (the stated purpose) but also sort students into future social roles and teach conformity to institutional rules. What terms apply to each function?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Manifest function**: transmitting academic knowledge — this is the intended, recognized purpose of schooling. **Latent function**: sorting students into social roles and teaching conformity to institutional rules — these are unintended (or at least unstated) consequences. From a conflict theory perspective, these latent functions would be interpreted as mechanisms for reproducing social inequality (advantaged students are sorted into better tracks, and conformity serves the interests of those in power).
</details>

**Q5.** A researcher argues that the concept of "mental illness" is not a fixed biological reality but rather a label applied by society to behaviors that deviate from culturally specific norms — and that what counts as a "disorder" shifts over time and across cultures. What theoretical perspective does this represent?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Social constructionism** (within the Symbolic Interactionist tradition). Social constructionism holds that categories like mental illness, race, and gender are not objective natural facts but rather meanings created through social interaction and institutionalized over time. Evidence: homosexuality was removed from the DSM in 1973; "drapetomania" (enslaved people's desire to flee) was once listed as a disorder. The label changes with social context, not just biology, supporting the constructionist view.
</details>

# Social Influence
**AAMC Category:** 7B — Social processes that influence human behavior | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Social influence refers to how the presence, behavior, and expectations of others shape individual attitudes and actions. Three landmark experiments anchor MCAT testing: Asch (conformity), Milgram (obedience to authority), and Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (role assignment and deindividuation). These studies reveal that situational forces — not just personality — powerfully determine behavior.

## High-Yield Facts
**Conformity (Asch, 1951):**
- Participants matched group's clearly wrong line-length answers ~37% of the time
- Conformity increases with group size up to ~4–5 people; levels off after that
- Conformity decreases if even one other person dissents (social support breaks conformity)
- **Normative social influence**: conforming to be liked/accepted (publicly comply, privately disagree)
- **Informational social influence**: conforming because you believe the group knows better (actually change private belief)

**Obedience to Authority (Milgram, 1963):**
- 65% of participants administered apparent maximum voltage (450V) to screaming confederate
- Obedience increases with: visible authority symbols (lab coat), physical proximity of authority figure, distance from the victim
- Obedience decreases with: peer rebellion, authority figure leaving the room, victim in same room

**Stanford Prison Experiment (Zimbardo, 1971):**
- Normal college students randomly assigned as "guards" or "prisoners" rapidly adopted roles
- Guards became abusive; prisoners became passive/depressed; study stopped after 6 days
- Demonstrates **deindividuation** and **role assignment** as powerful behavioral determinants

**Bystander Effect (Latané & Darley):**
- More bystanders present → less likely any individual will help (and help is slower)
- **Diffusion of responsibility**: each person assumes others will act
- **Pluralistic ignorance**: everyone looks to others to define the situation; if no one reacts, each person assumes it is not an emergency
- Kitty Genovese case: 38 witnesses, no one called police (though later reporting disputed the exact facts)

**Other Key Concepts:**
- **Groupthink**: group prioritizes consensus/unanimity over critical evaluation; illusion of invulnerability, self-censorship, pressure on dissenters
- **Social loafing**: individuals exert less effort in groups than when working alone (diffusion of effort)
- **Deindividuation**: reduced self-awareness and personal responsibility in group settings → increased likelihood of anti-normative behavior
- **Social facilitation**: presence of others improves performance on simple/well-learned tasks but impairs performance on novel/complex tasks

## Mnemonic
**"ABCD of Social Influence"**: Asch = conformity, Bystander = diffusion of responsibility, Conformity types = Normative vs. Informational, Deindividuation = loss of self in groups
**Bystander rule:** "1 bystander = most help. 100 bystanders = least help per person."

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
More bystanders = LESS help per person. 1 bystander = most likely to help. 100 bystanders = everyone assumes someone else will act. Diffusion of responsibility is the key mechanism. On MCAT passages, watch for large crowds at emergencies — the answer explaining why no one helped is always diffusion of responsibility (or pluralistic ignorance), never "people are cruel." Situational explanations dominate MCAT Psych/Soc.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Normative social influence = conforming to be LIKED or ACCEPTED — you publicly comply but privately still disagree. Informational social influence = conforming because you BELIEVE the group knows better — you actually change your private belief. MCAT distinguishes these by whether private belief changes. If the person still secretly disagrees, it's normative. If they genuinely adopt the group's view, it's informational.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** In Milgram's obedience experiment, what percentage of participants administered the maximum shock, and what factor most increased obedience?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**65% administered the maximum 450V shock.** The factor that most increased obedience was the **presence of a visible authority figure** (experimenter in a lab coat giving firm instructions). Obedience also increased when the victim (confederate) was in another room (distance from victim reduced emotional impact). When the experimenter left the room or peers rebelled, obedience dropped dramatically. The key lesson: authority symbols and physical/social distance from the victim powerfully override personal moral judgment.
</details>

**Q2.** A woman collapses on a busy city sidewalk surrounded by 50 passersby. No one stops to help for several minutes. What two social psychological phenomena explain this outcome?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Diffusion of responsibility** and **pluralistic ignorance**. Diffusion of responsibility: each bystander assumes someone else will or already has called for help, so no individual feels personally obligated. Pluralistic ignorance: each bystander looks to others for cues about how to interpret the situation; since no one is reacting, each person privately interprets the situation as non-emergency (even if concerned), reinforcing collective inaction. Together, these explain the bystander effect.
</details>

**Q3.** A jury deliberates for hours and ultimately reaches a unanimous verdict — despite several jurors privately having significant doubts. The group had not seriously considered the possibility that the defendant was innocent. What phenomenon does this illustrate?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Groupthink.** The group prioritized unanimity and consensus over thorough, critical evaluation of the evidence. Key features present: illusion of unanimity (verdict appeared unanimous but masked private doubts), self-censorship (doubting jurors suppressed disagreement), and absence of dissent that might have triggered proper deliberation. Groupthink is most common in cohesive groups under pressure to reach agreement.
</details>

**Q4.** Students working alone solve 20 math problems in an hour. The same students working in a group solve only 14 problems total — far less than their combined individual output. What phenomenon is this, and why does it occur?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Social loafing.** In group settings, individuals exert less effort than when working alone because individual contributions are less identifiable and accountability is diffused. Each group member unconsciously (or consciously) reduces their effort, assuming others will compensate. Social loafing is distinct from groupthink (poor decisions) and diffusion of responsibility (failure to help in emergencies) — it specifically refers to reduced individual effort on tasks.
</details>

**Q5.** A medical student privately disagrees with the senior resident's diagnosis but publicly agrees with it during rounds, not wanting to appear incompetent in front of the team. What type of social influence is operating, and does the student's private belief change?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Normative social influence** — the student conforms publicly to gain approval and avoid negative social consequences (appearing incompetent), but private belief does NOT change. The student still privately believes the diagnosis is wrong. Contrast with informational social influence, where the student would genuinely update their private belief because they concluded the resident must know better. The defining feature: normative = public compliance without private acceptance.
</details>

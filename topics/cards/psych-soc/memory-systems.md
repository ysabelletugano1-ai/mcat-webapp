# Memory Systems
**AAMC Category:** 6B — Making sense of the environment | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Memory involves three sequential processes: encoding (getting information in), storage (retaining it), and retrieval (getting it back out). The three-box (modal) model organizes memory into sensory, short-term (working), and long-term stores, each with distinct capacity and duration characteristics. Understanding brain structures and forgetting mechanisms is essential for MCAT Psych/Soc.

## High-Yield Facts
**Three-Box Model:**
- **Sensory memory**: very brief (milliseconds to seconds), large capacity; iconic (visual) and echoic (auditory)
- **Short-term memory (STM) / working memory**: 7±2 items ("Miller's Law"), ~20–30 seconds without rehearsal, limited capacity
- **Long-term memory (LTM)**: unlimited capacity and duration

**Types of Long-Term Memory:**
- **Explicit (declarative)**: conscious, intentional recall
  - *Episodic*: personal autobiographical events ("my graduation")
  - *Semantic*: general facts and knowledge ("Paris is the capital of France")
- **Implicit (non-declarative)**: unconscious, automatic
  - *Procedural*: motor skills and habits (riding a bike)
  - *Priming*: prior exposure influences response
  - *Conditioning*: classically or operantly conditioned responses

**Encoding:**
- **Elaborative rehearsal**: connecting new info to existing knowledge → deeper encoding, better retention
- **Maintenance rehearsal**: simple repetition → shallow encoding, poor long-term retention

**Brain Structures:**
- **Hippocampus**: explicit LTM consolidation (new explicit memories)
- **Amygdala**: emotional memory (fear, flashbulb memories)
- **Cerebellum & basal ganglia**: procedural/implicit memory
- **Prefrontal cortex**: working memory and executive function

**Forgetting:**
- **Encoding failure**: information never properly encoded
- **Decay**: memory trace fades over time without use
- **Proactive interference**: OLD memories interfere with learning NEW information
- **Retroactive interference**: NEW memories interfere with recalling OLD information
- **Retrieval failure**: information is stored but cannot be accessed (tip-of-the-tongue)

## Mnemonic
**"Encode, Store, Retrieve — Every Student Remembers"**
Memory types: **"ESP = Episodic, Semantic, Procedural"**
Interference: **"Pro = Old blocks New (PROactive = PRiOr). Retro = New blocks Old (RETROactive = REcent Trounces Old)"**

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: r/MCAT 520+ guides*
Hippocampus = Hotel for explicit memories (checked in for long-term storage). Damaged hippocampus → anterograde amnesia (can't form NEW explicit memories; old memories intact). H.M. had bilateral hippocampectomy → could learn procedural tasks (implicit, cerebellum) but could not form new episodic or semantic memories. This distinction — explicit vs. implicit memory intact after hippocampal damage — is a classic MCAT scenario.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Anterograde amnesia (can't form NEW memories after brain injury) vs. retrograde amnesia (can't recall OLD memories from before the injury). H.M. = anterograde amnesia from bilateral hippocampectomy. These terms are frequently swapped on MCAT — anchor "anterograde" to "forward in time" (can't make new) and "retrograde" to "backward in time" (can't access old).

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A patient suffers a stroke affecting the hippocampus. He can learn new motor skills with practice but cannot recall events from the day before. He remembers his childhood clearly. What type of amnesia does he have, and which memory systems are preserved vs. impaired?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Anterograde amnesia.** He cannot form NEW explicit (declarative) memories — evident from forgetting recent events. However, his implicit/procedural memory is intact (can learn new motor skills — cerebellum/basal ganglia are unaffected). Remote episodic memories (childhood) are preserved because they were consolidated before the stroke. This is the classic H.M. presentation.
</details>

**Q2.** A student studied French in high school and is now learning Spanish. She finds that her Spanish learning is interfering with her ability to recall her old French vocabulary. What type of forgetting is this?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Retroactive interference.** The NEW learning (Spanish) is interfering with recall of OLD memories (French). Retroactive = recent/new memories trounce old ones. (Contrast with proactive interference, where old French would interfere with learning Spanish — old blocks new.)
</details>

**Q3.** A researcher finds that participants who were asked to explain HOW new vocabulary words relate to their own personal experiences remember far more words than participants who simply repeated the words aloud 20 times. What encoding principle explains this?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Elaborative rehearsal produces deeper encoding than maintenance rehearsal.** Connecting new information to existing knowledge or personal experience (levels of processing — deep/semantic encoding) creates stronger, more durable memory traces than simple repetition (shallow/phonological encoding). This is why self-referential encoding and meaningful association outperform rote repetition.
</details>

**Q4.** During a traumatic car accident, a person experiences intense fear. Years later, they have a vivid, detailed memory of the accident — far more detailed than memories of ordinary events from the same period. What brain structure and memory phenomenon explain this?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Flashbulb memory, mediated by the amygdala.** The amygdala enhances consolidation of emotionally charged memories by modulating hippocampal activity. Intense fear/stress → amygdala activation → stronger memory encoding. Flashbulb memories are vivid, detailed recollections of emotionally significant events (though they are not always perfectly accurate despite their vividness).
</details>

**Q5.** What is the molecular basis of long-term memory formation at the synaptic level, and which structure is primarily responsible for initiating it?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Long-term potentiation (LTP)**, primarily initiated in the **hippocampus**. LTP involves repeated stimulation of a synapse → increased sensitivity of postsynaptic receptors (especially NMDA receptors) → stronger synaptic connections → structural changes (more receptors, dendritic spine growth). LTP is the leading cellular mechanism for learning and memory consolidation. The phrase "neurons that fire together, wire together" (Hebb's rule) captures the principle.
</details>

# Health Disparities & Social Determinants of Health
**AAMC Category:** 10A — Social inequality | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Health outcomes are not equally distributed across society — they are systematically shaped by social, economic, and environmental conditions. Social determinants of health (SDOH) explain why populations differ in disease rates, life expectancy, and access to care. Socioeconomic status (SES) is the single strongest predictor of health globally. Understanding these structural factors — not individual choices — is central to MCAT Psych/Soc reasoning.

## High-Yield Facts
**Social Determinants of Health (SDOH):**
- Income and SES (strongest predictor)
- Education level
- Race/ethnicity (independent of SES — reflects structural racism)
- Neighborhood and physical environment (pollution, green space, crime)
- Access to healthcare (insurance, proximity, affordability)
- Social support networks
- Employment and working conditions

**Social Gradient in Health:**
- Health improves at every step up the SES ladder — not just at the poverty line
- Even affluent people have worse health than the very wealthiest; the gradient is continuous
- Mechanism: SES affects stress exposure, healthcare access, health behaviors, environmental exposures

**Health Disparity vs. Health Care Disparity:**
- **Health disparity**: differences in health OUTCOMES between groups (e.g., Black Americans have higher rates of hypertension, lower life expectancy)
- **Health care disparity**: differences in ACCESS to or QUALITY of care between groups (e.g., Black patients less likely to receive adequate pain treatment)
- These are related but distinct concepts — MCAT distinguishes them

**Sick Role (Parsons):**
- Social role adopted by ill individuals; expectations and privileges
- Patient is: (1) exempt from normal social responsibilities, (2) not blamed for being ill
- Patient must: (1) want to get well, (2) seek competent help and cooperate with treatment
- Criticism: ignores chronic illness (you can't always "get well"), ignores patient agency

**Medicalization:**
- The process by which non-medical conditions are defined and treated as medical problems
- Examples: ADHD, obesity, alcoholism, childbirth, aging, grief
- Creates a medical framework for behaviors/states that were previously seen as moral, social, or personal issues
- Related concept: **pharmaceuticalization** (treating social problems with drugs)

**Social Epidemiology:**
- Studies the distribution of disease in populations and focuses on social factors as causes
- Uses concepts: incidence (new cases), prevalence (existing cases), morbidity (disease burden), mortality (death)

## Mnemonic
**SDOH anchor: "INCOME matters"** = Income, Neighborhood, Community support, Occupation, Medical access, Education
**Health vs. Healthcare disparity:** "Outcomes vs. Access" — Health = Outcomes, Healthcare = Access to care

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
For ANY health disparity question on the MCAT: anchor on power, access, and structural barriers. The MCAT answer is almost never "individuals make unhealthy choices" — it's always "structural/systemic factors limit access or create exposure." When a passage describes racial health disparities, the correct interpretation is structural racism, residential segregation, differential access to care, and chronic stress from discrimination — not genetics or individual behavior. This framing is consistent across all AAMC materials.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Health disparity ≠ health care disparity. **Health disparity** = different health OUTCOMES (e.g., life expectancy, disease rates). **Health care disparity** = different access to or quality of CARE (e.g., who gets referred for surgery, who receives adequate pain management). A community can have excellent healthcare access but still experience health disparities due to upstream SDOH (poverty, pollution). Conversely, communities may have similar disease burdens but differ in access to treatment. MCAT passages will distinguish these — read carefully.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A public health researcher finds that even among college-educated, insured patients, those with lower household incomes have higher rates of Type 2 diabetes than those with higher incomes — even after controlling for diet and exercise. What concept best explains this finding?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The social gradient in health** (and the role of **chronic stress/allostatic load** as a mechanism). The social gradient in health refers to the continuous, stepwise relationship between SES and health outcomes — higher SES = better health, and this gradient exists even among people who are not in poverty. Even within "educated, insured" populations, income differences produce health differences. Mechanisms include chronic psychological stress (financial strain → elevated cortisol → metabolic dysfunction), neighborhood differences in food access and environmental exposures, and social capital differences.
</details>

**Q2.** A 45-year-old man with chronic back pain is told by his doctor: "I know you're in a lot of pain, but you need to try to return to work as soon as possible and keep up with your physical therapy." According to Parsons' sick role model, is this appropriate? What obligations does the patient have?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Yes, this is consistent with Parsons' sick role model.** The sick role grants two rights: exemption from normal responsibilities (not expected to work through illness) and not being blamed for being sick. In return, the patient has two obligations: (1) want to recover and (2) seek competent help and cooperate with treatment (physical therapy). The doctor is reinforcing the patient's obligations — to try to recover and comply with treatment. However, critics of Parsons' model note it poorly fits chronic conditions where full recovery may be impossible.
</details>

**Q3.** Which of the following is an example of medicalization: (A) A doctor diagnosing pneumonia and prescribing antibiotics, (B) Defining childhood hyperactivity and inattention as "ADHD" requiring medication and behavioral therapy, (C) A nurse teaching a patient about healthy diet choices.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**(B) — ADHD is the classic example of medicalization.** Medicalization is the process by which non-medical conditions come to be defined, understood, and treated as medical problems. ADHD represents behavior patterns (inattention, hyperactivity) that were once viewed as personality traits, discipline problems, or childhood norms, now redefined as a medical disorder requiring diagnosis and treatment. (A) is standard medical care for an established infectious disease — not medicalization. (C) is health education, not defining a new medical category.
</details>

**Q4.** Research shows that Black patients are significantly less likely than white patients to be prescribed adequate pain medication for the same reported pain levels. Is this an example of a health disparity or a health care disparity? Explain.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Health care disparity.** This is a difference in the QUALITY of CARE received — specifically, differential treatment by providers based on race. The disparity exists at the point of care delivery, not in underlying disease burden. Contrast with a health disparity, which would be if Black patients had higher rates of a disease (different health OUTCOME). Note that health care disparities can lead to health disparities over time — undertreated pain leads to worse outcomes. But the described phenomenon (differential prescribing for the same pain) is specifically a healthcare disparity.
</details>

**Q5.** A researcher studying a low-income urban neighborhood finds that residents have higher rates of asthma, obesity, and depression than residents of wealthier neighborhoods nearby. Which social determinants of health are most likely contributing to these differences?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Multiple SDOH are converging:** (1) **Neighborhood environment** — low-income urban areas often have higher air pollution (near highways/industry), fewer green spaces, and greater exposure to environmental toxins → asthma. (2) **Food environment** — food deserts with limited access to fresh produce → obesity. (3) **Chronic stress from poverty and neighborhood insecurity** — chronic psychosocial stress activates HPA axis → metabolic dysfunction (obesity) and depression. (4) **Limited healthcare access** — less insurance coverage, fewer nearby providers → undertreatment of all three conditions. (5) **Social isolation and lack of social capital** → amplifies depression. These factors interact and compound each other.
</details>

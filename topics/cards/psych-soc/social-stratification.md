# Social Stratification & Mobility
**AAMC Category:** 10A — Social inequality | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Social stratification is the hierarchical ranking of individuals and groups in society based on wealth, power, and prestige. Every society has some form of stratification, but systems differ dramatically in how much movement is possible between levels. Understanding types of stratification systems, forms of social mobility, and the role of cultural and social capital is essential for MCAT Psych/Soc, particularly for analyzing health disparities and inequality.

## High-Yield Facts
**Systems of Stratification:**
- **Class system** (open): position based on achieved status; social mobility is possible (most modern societies)
- **Caste system** (closed): position determined by birth (ascribed status); mobility is extremely rare; enforced by tradition, religion, or law (e.g., India's historical caste system, apartheid South Africa)
- **Slavery**: forced labor with no rights; extreme form of closed stratification
- **Estate/feudal system**: nobles, clergy, commoners; mobility rare; historical Europe

**Types of Social Mobility:**
- **Vertical mobility**: movement UP or DOWN the social hierarchy (e.g., promotion → upward; bankruptcy → downward)
- **Horizontal mobility**: movement to a similar social position in a different setting (e.g., switching jobs at the same pay level)
- **Intragenerational mobility**: status change within a single person's own lifetime
- **Intergenerational mobility**: status change across generations — comparing parent's status to child's status
- **Structural mobility**: mobility caused by broad societal shifts (e.g., industrialization creating new jobs) rather than individual effort

**Key Concepts in Inequality:**
- **Meritocracy**: the belief that social position is determined by individual ability and effort; contrast with reality where structural barriers (racism, wealth gaps, educational inequality) limit actual mobility even in "open" systems
- **Social capital**: networks, relationships, and connections that provide advantages (knowing the right people)
- **Cultural capital** (Bourdieu): non-financial social assets — cultural knowledge, behaviors, tastes, credentials, and communication styles that signal class membership and confer advantages (knowing how to navigate elite institutions, speaking in certain ways)
- **Symbolic capital** (Bourdieu): honor, prestige, and recognition that comes from social position

**Poverty:**
- **Absolute poverty**: income/resources below the minimum needed to meet basic survival needs (food, shelter, clothing)
- **Relative poverty**: income/resources significantly below the average standard of living in a given society — you can survive but cannot participate fully in society
- **Social exclusion**: marginalization from mainstream economic, social, and political life; inability to participate in normal social activities due to poverty or discrimination

**Social Reproduction:**
- The process by which social inequality is transmitted across generations (wealthy families pass on wealth, social capital, cultural capital → children maintain higher position)
- Explains why "open" class systems still show limited actual mobility

## Mnemonic
**Mobility types:** "VHII" = Vertical (up/down), Horizontal (same level), Intragenerational (within your lifetime), Intergenerational (across generations — parent vs. child)
**Stratification systems open/closed:** "Class = Could move. Caste = Can't move."
**Bourdieu's capitals:** "Social = Who you know. Cultural = What you know (and how you carry yourself). Economic = What you own."

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
Open system ≠ easy mobility — just POSSIBLE. Caste system = mobility nearly impossible. MCAT Psych/Soc tests that even "open" class systems have structural barriers — systemic racism, wealth gaps, differential educational quality, and cultural capital advantages — that limit actual upward mobility. When MCAT passages describe someone achieving upward mobility "through hard work alone," the MCAT expects you to recognize what structural advantages or barriers were present. The correct analytical frame is structural, not purely individualistic.

## Common Trap
⚠️ **Intergenerational mobility** = change in status between a parent and their child (ACROSS generations — different people). **Intragenerational mobility** = change in a single person's own status during their lifetime (WITHIN one generation — same person). The prefix "inter" (between) applies to different generations/people; "intra" (within) applies to the same person. Do not mix these. MCAT scenarios often describe one clearly (e.g., "a coal miner's daughter becomes a physician" = intergenerational upward mobility) — identify which by asking: same person or parent vs. child?

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A woman born into a working-class family works her way up from a factory floor job to plant manager over 25 years at the same company. Her daughter later attends medical school and becomes a physician. Classify each person's mobility by type (vertical/horizontal, intra/intergenerational).
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The woman (factory worker → plant manager):** Vertical upward mobility (she moved up the hierarchy) + Intragenerational mobility (this change happened within her own lifetime). **Her daughter (plant manager's child → physician):** Vertical upward mobility (physician is higher social status than plant manager) + Intergenerational mobility (the status change is being compared across generations — mother vs. daughter). The daughter's mobility is intergenerational because we are comparing her status to her parent's, not to her own prior status.
</details>

**Q2.** Pierre Bourdieu argues that children from upper-class families have advantages in elite universities beyond just money — including knowing how to speak to professors, familiarity with fine arts and literature, and comfort in formal institutional settings. What concept does this describe?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Cultural capital** (Bourdieu). Cultural capital includes the non-financial social assets — cultural knowledge, tastes, manners, communication styles, and educational credentials — that confer advantages in navigating social institutions. Upper-class children inherit cultural capital from their families that helps them succeed in educational and professional settings, independent of their economic resources. This is distinct from **social capital** (networks/connections) and **economic capital** (money/property). Cultural capital is a key mechanism of social reproduction.
</details>

**Q3.** A family's income puts them above the absolute poverty line — they can afford basic food and shelter — but they cannot afford school trips, holiday gifts, or social activities that most families in their community take for granted. What type of poverty does this describe?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Relative poverty.** The family meets their absolute basic needs (food, shelter) but falls significantly below the average living standard in their society, preventing full participation in normal social and economic life. Relative poverty is defined in relation to the surrounding society's living standards, not just bare survival needs. This family may also experience **social exclusion** — being marginalized from mainstream social participation. Relative poverty is harder to eliminate because as average living standards rise, the threshold rises too.
</details>

**Q4.** A sociologist argues that in the United States, a person's social class is largely determined by their family background — access to elite education, inherited wealth, neighborhood, and social connections — rather than purely individual talent and effort. What concept does this critique, and what does it support instead?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**This critiques meritocracy** (the belief that position is purely determined by individual merit/ability/effort) and supports a **structural view of inequality**. While the US class system is formally "open" (mobility is possible), structural barriers — inherited wealth, differential school quality, social capital networks, and systemic racism — mean that family background is a far stronger predictor of outcomes than individual merit alone. This is supported by data showing limited intergenerational mobility in the US compared to other wealthy nations. The structural critique does not say mobility never occurs — only that it is not purely meritocratic.
</details>

**Q5.** A major steel mill closes, eliminating thousands of jobs in a region. Many former steelworkers find new jobs in service industries, but at significantly lower wages and social prestige. What type of mobility is this, and what specific term describes mobility caused by large-scale economic shifts rather than individual choices?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Downward vertical mobility** — workers moved down the social hierarchy (lower wages, lower prestige). This is also an example of **structural mobility** (sometimes called structural displacement): the mobility was caused by a broad macroeconomic shift (deindustrialization, mill closure) rather than by individual decisions or failures. Structural mobility happens when the economy itself changes — new industries emerge or old ones collapse — forcing collective movement up or down the hierarchy regardless of individual ability or effort. This distinguishes it from exchange mobility (one person moves up as another moves down in a zero-sum sense).
</details>

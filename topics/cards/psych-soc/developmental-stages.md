# Developmental Stages
**AAMC Category:** 7A — Individual influences on behavior | **Section:** Psych/Soc | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-psych-soc-hub]]

## Core Concept
Developmental psychology tracks cognitive, psychosocial, and moral growth across the lifespan. Three theorists dominate MCAT testing: Piaget (cognitive stages), Erikson (psychosocial crises), and Kohlberg (moral reasoning levels). Each framework describes qualitatively distinct stages, and MCAT passages require identifying which stage applies to a described behavior or scenario.

## High-Yield Facts
**Piaget — Cognitive Development:**
- **Sensorimotor** (0–2 yrs): learning via senses and motor actions; object permanence develops ~8–12 months
- **Preoperational** (2–7 yrs): symbolic/language use; egocentrism (can't take others' perspectives); no conservation; animism; centration
- **Concrete Operational** (7–11 yrs): conservation (liquid, mass, number); reversibility; decentration; seriation; classification; but only with tangible/concrete objects
- **Formal Operational** (12+ yrs): abstract, hypothetical, and deductive reasoning; systematic problem-solving; metacognition

**Erikson — Psychosocial Development (8 stages — know first 5 for MCAT):**
| Stage | Age | Crisis | Positive Outcome |
|-------|-----|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | Infancy | Trust vs. Mistrust | Hope |
| 2 | Toddler (1–3) | Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt | Will |
| 3 | Preschool (3–6) | Initiative vs. Guilt | Purpose |
| 4 | School age (6–12) | Industry vs. Inferiority | Competence |
| 5 | Adolescence | Identity vs. Role Confusion | Fidelity |
| 6 | Young adult | Intimacy vs. Isolation | Love |
| 7 | Middle adult | Generativity vs. Stagnation | Care |
| 8 | Late adult | Ego Integrity vs. Despair | Wisdom |

**Kohlberg — Moral Development:**
- **Preconventional** (self-interest): Stage 1 = obedience/punishment avoidance; Stage 2 = self-interest/exchange
- **Conventional** (social norms): Stage 3 = good boy/girl, approval seeking; Stage 4 = law and order
- **Postconventional** (universal principles): Stage 5 = social contract; Stage 6 = universal ethical principles

**Vygotsky — Sociocultural Theory:**
- **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)**: gap between what a child can do alone vs. with guidance
- **Scaffolding**: temporary support from a more skilled person to help reach ZPD
- Emphasized social and cultural context in cognitive development (contrast with Piaget's individual discovery)

**Attachment (Bowlby/Ainsworth — Strange Situation):**
- **Secure**: distressed when caregiver leaves, comforted on return (most common, healthiest)
- **Anxious-Ambivalent (Resistant)**: very distressed when caregiver leaves, hard to comfort on return
- **Avoidant**: little distress when caregiver leaves, ignores on return
- **Disorganized**: inconsistent, disoriented behavior; associated with abuse/neglect

## Mnemonic
**Piaget stages:** "Silly People Can't Function" = Sensorimotor, Preoperational, Concrete Operational, Formal Operational
**Erikson (first 5):** "Trust Autonomy In Industry, Identity" = Trust, Autonomy, Initiative, Industry, Identity
**Kohlberg:** "Pre → Con → Post" = Self → Society → Principles

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
Piaget: Silly People Can't Function = Sensorimotor, Preoperational, Concrete, Formal. For Erikson, pair the stage with the age group, not just the conflict name — passages always describe an age and a behavioral struggle; map them together. Kohlberg: the KEY signal is the REASON given for a moral decision, not the decision itself. Someone can refuse to steal for any of the three levels — what matters is WHY (fear of punishment vs. social norms vs. universal principles).

## Common Trap
⚠️ Object permanence (knowing an object exists when hidden) develops in the **Sensorimotor stage** (~8–12 months). Before object permanence develops, "out of sight = out of existence." Do NOT confuse with conservation (liquid volume unchanged despite container shape change), which develops in the **Concrete Operational** stage (~7 years). These two milestones are the most frequently confused Piaget concepts on MCAT.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A 4-year-old watches a researcher pour water from a short, wide glass into a tall, narrow glass. When asked if the amount of water changed, she says "yes, there is more water now because it's taller." What Piaget stage is she in, and what concept is she lacking?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Preoperational stage (ages 2–7); she lacks conservation.** Conservation is the understanding that quantity remains the same despite changes in appearance. Preoperational children center on one perceptual dimension (height of the glass) and cannot reverse the transformation mentally. Conservation develops in the Concrete Operational stage (~7 years).
</details>

**Q2.** A 16-year-old is struggling with questions like "Who am I?" and "What do I want to do with my life?" He tries on different social roles and friend groups. According to Erikson, what psychosocial stage is he navigating?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Identity vs. Role Confusion** (Stage 5, Adolescence). Erikson's fifth stage involves the central task of forming a coherent personal identity — integrating past roles, values, and social feedback into a stable self-concept. Successfully resolving this stage leads to fidelity (a sense of purpose and commitment). Failure leads to role confusion and identity diffusion.
</details>

**Q3.** A child is asked why a character in a story chose not to steal even though he could have gotten away with it. The child says, "Because stealing is against the rules and laws that keep our society working." What Kohlberg level and stage does this reflect?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Conventional level, Stage 4 (Law and Order).** The child's reasoning focuses on maintaining social order and obeying established laws — not self-interest (Preconventional) and not abstract universal principles (Postconventional). Stage 4 reasoning emphasizes duty, respect for authority, and preserving the social system.
</details>

**Q4.** A teacher notices that a student can solve 2-digit multiplication problems with a little guidance from an adult, but cannot solve them alone. The teacher provides structured hints that fade as the student gains skill. What Vygotsky concepts are illustrated?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) and scaffolding.** ZPD = the range between what the student can do independently (can't yet solve 2-digit multiplication alone) and what they can do with guidance (can solve with adult help). Scaffolding = the temporary, adjustable support the teacher provides, which is gradually withdrawn as competence develops. Vygotsky emphasized that learning is fundamentally social.
</details>

**Q5.** In Ainsworth's Strange Situation, a toddler becomes extremely distressed when her mother leaves but, upon her mother's return, pushes her away and cannot be soothed — alternating between wanting contact and rejecting it. What attachment style does this describe?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Anxious-Ambivalent (Resistant) attachment.** The child shows high distress at separation but paradoxically resists comfort upon reunion — reflecting preoccupation with the attachment figure combined with anger or ambivalence. This is associated with inconsistent caregiving (sometimes responsive, sometimes not). Contrast with avoidant (minimizes distress, ignores caregiver on return) and secure (distressed but easily soothed on return).
</details>

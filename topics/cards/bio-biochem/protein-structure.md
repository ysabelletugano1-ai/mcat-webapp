# Protein Structure & Function
**AAMC Category:** 1A | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
Protein structure is organized into four levels, each stabilized by different forces. Function depends on three-dimensional shape, which can be disrupted by denaturing agents. The MCAT tests which bonds are broken at each level and how amino acid properties determine structure.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Primary**: amino acid sequence; held by peptide bonds (covalent); NOT broken by denaturation
- **Secondary**: α-helix and β-pleated sheet; held by H-bonds between backbone atoms
- **Tertiary**: overall 3D fold; held by H-bonds, hydrophobic interactions, disulfide bonds (covalent), ionic bonds, van der Waals forces
- **Quaternary**: multiple subunits; same forces as tertiary between subunits
- **Denaturation breaks secondary, tertiary, quaternary — NOT primary**
- **Hydrophobic core**: nonpolar R groups buried inside; polar/charged on surface (water-soluble proteins)
- **Disulfide bonds**: only covalent bond in tertiary structure; formed between two Cys residues; broken by reducing agents
- **Chaperones**: assist proper folding; prevent misfolding aggregates

## Mnemonic
**"Primary Peptides, Secondary H-bonds, Tertiary R-groups, Quaternary = More"**

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: r/MCAT 520+ study guide*
Denaturation question shortcut: **Denaturation breaks everything except primary structure (peptide bonds).** Heat, urea, detergents, extreme pH → lose secondary/tertiary/quaternary. Add reducing agents → also break disulfide bonds. Renaturation is possible only if primary structure intact and conditions return to normal.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Students confuse "denaturation" with "destruction." Denatured protein still has its amino acid sequence (primary structure) intact — it's just unfolded. Renature it under the right conditions and function can return.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** Urea disrupts hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions. Which levels of protein structure are affected by urea treatment?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Secondary, tertiary, and quaternary.** Primary structure (peptide bonds) is covalent and not broken by urea. Urea unfolds the protein by disrupting H-bonds (secondary) and hydrophobic packing (tertiary). Quaternary structure is also disrupted as subunits separate.
</details>

**Q2.** A protein is treated with β-mercaptoethanol (a reducing agent) and then urea. What additional structure is disrupted compared to urea alone?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Disulfide bonds (tertiary structure).** β-mercaptoethanol reduces the S–S bonds between cysteine residues. These are the only covalent bonds in tertiary structure. Urea handles H-bonds and hydrophobics; the reducing agent handles disulfides.
</details>

**Q3.** A membrane protein has a transmembrane α-helical domain. Which amino acid R-group properties are most likely found in the transmembrane segment?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Nonpolar/hydrophobic R groups** (e.g., Leu, Val, Ile, Phe). The lipid bilayer interior is hydrophobic. Transmembrane segments must be nonpolar to be stable in the membrane. Charged or polar residues would be energetically unfavorable in the lipid core.
</details>

**Q4.** A protein normally functions as a homodimer. A mutation disrupts the hydrophobic patch at the dimer interface. What level of protein structure is directly affected?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Quaternary structure.** The interaction between two subunits (homodimer) is quaternary. The hydrophobic patch at the interface is what holds the two monomers together. The individual monomers' tertiary structure may remain intact.
</details>

**Q5.** Which of the following correctly describes the role of molecular chaperones in protein folding?
A) They provide the energy for peptide bond formation
B) They prevent premature or incorrect folding during synthesis
C) They add post-translational modifications to ensure proper structure
D) They cleave misfolded proteins for degradation
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**B.** Chaperones (e.g., HSP70, GroEL) bind to hydrophobic regions of nascent polypeptides to prevent them from aggregating or misfolding before the full sequence is synthesized. They do not form peptide bonds (ribosomes do), add modifications (that's kinases, glycosyltransferases, etc.), or degrade proteins (proteasomes do that).
</details>

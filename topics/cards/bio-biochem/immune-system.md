# Immune System
**AAMC Category:** 3B | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
The immune system has two arms: innate immunity (rapid, nonspecific, no memory) and adaptive immunity (slower, antigen-specific, forms immunological memory). Adaptive immunity is mediated by T cells (cell-mediated) and B cells (humoral/antibody-based). MHC molecules are the critical link between antigen presentation and T cell activation.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Innate immunity (first line, immediate)**: physical barriers (skin, mucus) → innate cells: neutrophils (first responders, phagocytosis, release toxic granules), macrophages (phagocytosis, antigen presentation, release cytokines like IL-1, TNF), NK cells (kill virus-infected and cancer cells without prior sensitization), complement system (opsonization, MAC pore formation, inflammation)
- **Adaptive immunity (days to weeks, memory-forming)**: T cells (cell-mediated) and B cells (humoral/antibody)
- **MHC I**: expressed on ALL nucleated cells; presents endogenous (intracellular) antigens (viral proteins, tumor antigens); recognized by CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) → kill infected/cancerous cells; "8 ÷ 1 = 8" mnemonic
- **MHC II**: expressed ONLY on professional APCs (macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells); presents exogenous (extracellular) antigens; recognized by CD4⁺ helper T cells → activate immune response; "4 × 2 = 8" mnemonic
- **T cell activation**: naive T cell must receive signal 1 (TCR binds MHC-antigen complex) AND signal 2 (CD28 binds B7 on APC) → without signal 2 = anergy (T cell tolerance)
- **CD4⁺ helper T cells**: activate macrophages (Th1), stimulate B cells to make antibodies (Th2), regulate inflammation; essential for coordinating both arms of adaptive immunity
- **CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells**: kill cells displaying abnormal MHC I; release perforin and granzymes → target cell apoptosis
- **B cells and antibodies**: clonal selection → activated B cell → plasma cell → secretes antibodies; IgM (first response, pentamer), IgG (most abundant, crosses placenta, secondary response), IgA (secretions: saliva, breast milk, gut), IgE (allergy and parasites), IgD (B cell surface receptor)
- **Clonal selection**: antigen binds specific lymphocyte receptor → that clone proliferates (clonal expansion) → effector cells + memory cells
- **Active immunity**: host generates own immune response to antigen (natural infection or vaccination) → memory cells formed → long-lasting
- **Passive immunity**: pre-formed antibodies transferred (maternal IgG via placenta/breast milk; anti-venom; immunoglobulin therapy) → immediate protection, NO memory cells, short-lived

## Mnemonic
**MHC I × CD8 = 1×8=8; MHC II × CD4 = 2×4=8** — Both products equal 8. Use this to remember the pairing: MHC I → CD8⁺ CTLs; MHC II → CD4⁺ helper T cells.

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: r/MCAT 520+ study sheet*
MHC I × CD8 = 1×8=8. MHC II × CD4 = 2×4=8. Both products equal 8 — use this to remember the pairing. For the rest: MHC I is on ALL nucleated cells presenting INTERNAL threats (viral); MHC II is on APC-only presenting EXTERNAL threats (bacteria engulfed). Think: I = Inside threats; II = Ingested/external threats.

## Common Trap
⚠️ MHC I is on ALL NUCLEATED cells — but red blood cells (RBCs) have no nucleus and therefore express NO MHC I. This is why RBCs are not targeted by cytotoxic T cells even when infected with intracellular parasites (like malaria). Platelets also lack MHC I for the same reason.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A cell is infected by a virus and begins producing viral proteins intracellularly. Which MHC class will present these antigens, and which T cell population will respond?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**MHC Class I will present the viral peptides, and CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) will respond.** Intracellular proteins (including viral proteins synthesized inside the infected cell) are degraded by proteasomes → peptides transported into ER via TAP → loaded onto MHC I → displayed on cell surface. CD8⁺ CTLs recognize the MHC I-peptide complex via their TCR → release perforin and granzymes → infected cell undergoes apoptosis. This is the cell-mediated response to intracellular pathogens and tumors.
</details>

**Q2.** A child receives the tetanus vaccine (inactivated toxin). Years later, the child is exposed to tetanus bacteria. Compare the speed and magnitude of the immune response to the first vaccination versus this re-exposure.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Re-exposure triggers a much faster (within hours to days vs. 1-2 weeks) and much larger secondary immune response.** The first vaccination (primary response): naive B and T cells are activated → clonal expansion → effector cells + memory cells; takes 1-2 weeks to peak; predominantly IgM early then class switching to IgG. Re-exposure (secondary response): memory B cells and T cells respond rapidly → large IgG surge with higher affinity (affinity maturation from germinal centers); the response is faster, larger, and more sustained, providing effective protection. This is the basis of vaccination — priming immunological memory without causing disease.
</details>

**Q3.** Explain clonal selection and why it is essential for a specific adaptive immune response.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Clonal selection is the process by which a specific antigen selects and activates only the lymphocyte clone(s) bearing a complementary receptor, causing that clone to proliferate massively.** The adaptive immune system maintains a diverse repertoire of B and T cells, each with a unique receptor generated by V(D)J recombination. When an antigen enters, it binds only to lymphocytes whose receptors match it — those cells receive activation signals and expand into thousands of identical effector cells (clonal expansion). Cells that don't match are not stimulated. This ensures specificity: only the "right" cells respond to a given pathogen. After infection, some expanded clones persist as memory cells.
</details>

**Q4.** A newborn has no functional T or B cells (severe combined immunodeficiency, SCID). Which pathogens pose the greatest immediate threat — extracellular bacteria, intracellular viruses, or both — and why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Both pose grave threats, but for different reasons.** Without T cells: no CTLs to kill virus-infected cells, no helper T cells to activate macrophages or B cells. Without B cells: no antibodies for opsonization, complement activation, or neutralization of extracellular pathogens. The innate immune system (neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells, complement) provides some immediate defense, but without adaptive immunity, even normally benign opportunistic infections are lethal. Additionally, without T cell help, B cells cannot class-switch from IgM to IgG/IgA or undergo affinity maturation — antibody responses are weak even if some B cells existed. SCID infants require bone marrow transplant for reconstitution.
</details>

**Q5.** A patient receives a rattlesnake antivenom (purified antibodies from immunized horses). Is this active or passive immunity? Will the patient have long-term protection against future rattlesnake bites?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**This is passive immunity — the patient receives pre-formed antibodies and does NOT generate their own immune response.** Antivenom works immediately (antibodies are already present, no lag time), which is essential for acute envenomation. However, because the patient's own lymphocytes were not activated and no clonal expansion occurred, NO memory cells are formed. Protection is short-lived (weeks to months, until the transferred antibodies are degraded). Future rattlesnake bites would require another dose of antivenom. Contrast with active immunity (vaccination or natural infection): slower onset, but generates memory → long-lasting protection.
</details>

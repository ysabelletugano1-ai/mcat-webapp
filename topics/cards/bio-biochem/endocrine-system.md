# Endocrine System & Hormones
**AAMC Category:** 3A | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
Hormones are chemical messengers that regulate physiology via two major mechanisms: steroid hormones (lipid-soluble, intracellular receptors, gene-level effects) and peptide hormones (water-soluble, surface receptors, second messenger cascades). The hypothalamus-pituitary axis coordinates hormonal output through hierarchical control and negative feedback.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Steroid hormones**: derived from cholesterol; lipid-soluble → cross plasma membrane freely; bind intracellular (cytoplasmic or nuclear) receptors; hormone-receptor complex acts as transcription factor → alter gene expression; **slow onset, long duration**; examples: cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, calcitriol (vitamin D)
- **Peptide/protein hormones**: water-soluble → cannot cross membrane; bind cell-surface receptors (GPCRs or receptor tyrosine kinases); activate second messenger cascades (cAMP via adenylyl cyclase; IP₃/Ca²⁺ via phospholipase C); **fast onset, short duration**; examples: insulin, glucagon, GH, FSH, LH, TSH, ADH, oxytocin, PTH
- **Thyroid hormone (T3/T4) — MCAT exception**: synthesized from tyrosine (looks peptide-like) but is lipid-soluble → crosses membrane → binds intracellular receptor → acts like steroid (gene-level effects, slow); this is a classic trick question
- **Hypothalamus-pituitary axis**: hypothalamus → releasing hormones → anterior pituitary → tropic hormones → target gland → hormones → negative feedback inhibits hypothalamus and pituitary
- **Insulin** (beta cells, pancreas): released in FED state; ↓blood glucose via ↑glucose uptake (GLUT4 in muscle/fat), glycogenesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis; inhibits glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis
- **Glucagon** (alpha cells, pancreas): released in FASTED state; ↑blood glucose via glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis (liver); opposes insulin
- **Cortisol** (adrenal cortex, steroid): stress hormone; ↑blood glucose (gluconeogenesis), anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive; chronically elevated in Cushing syndrome
- **Aldosterone** (adrenal cortex, steroid): ↑Na⁺ reabsorption (collecting duct) and ↑K⁺ excretion → water follows Na⁺ → ↑blood volume and pressure; regulated by RAAS
- **ADH/Vasopressin** (posterior pituitary, peptide): ↑water reabsorption in collecting duct (inserts aquaporins); ↑blood osmolarity or ↓volume → ADH release → concentrated urine

## Mnemonic
**"Steroids Speak Slowly to the DNA; Peptides are Quick with a Second-message Relay"** — Steroids = slow gene effects; Peptides = fast second messengers.

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: MedSchoolCoach MCAT*
Steroid = gene-level effects (slow). Peptide = second messenger effects (fast). Thyroid hormone LOOKS like it should be peptide (made from tyrosine) but ACTS like steroid (lipid-soluble, intracellular receptor). This is a classic MCAT trick. When asked about thyroid hormone mechanism, always answer: intracellular receptor, gene transcription — same as a steroid despite its amino acid origin.

## Common Trap
⚠️ ADH (antidiuretic hormone) increases water RETENTION — it makes you urinate LESS, producing more concentrated urine. Students confuse ADH with a diuretic because of the word "diuretic" in the name. ADH = ANTI-diuretic = keep water in the body. Absence of ADH (diabetes insipidus) → dilute, voluminous urine.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A researcher adds a steroid hormone and a peptide hormone to two separate cell cultures. Which hormone would you expect to produce a measurable response faster, and what accounts for the difference in speed?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The peptide hormone produces a faster response.** Peptide hormones bind surface receptors → activate pre-existing signaling proteins (G proteins, adenylyl cyclase, protein kinases) → effects occur within seconds to minutes (e.g., enzyme activation, ion channel opening). Steroid hormones must cross the membrane, bind intracellular receptors, translocate to the nucleus, alter gene transcription, then await protein synthesis — a process requiring hours. The tradeoff: steroid effects last longer because newly synthesized proteins persist.
</details>

**Q2.** A patient is found to have very high TSH but low T3/T4 levels. Where is the likely defect, and what does this tell you about the feedback axis?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The defect is in the thyroid gland itself (primary hypothyroidism).** In the HPT axis: hypothalamus → TRH → anterior pituitary → TSH → thyroid gland → T3/T4 → negative feedback on both hypothalamus and pituitary. If T3/T4 is low, negative feedback is reduced → pituitary is "released" → secretes excess TSH trying to stimulate the failing thyroid. High TSH + low T3/T4 = primary hypothyroidism (e.g., Hashimoto's thyroiditis). If TSH were also low, the defect would be at the pituitary (secondary) or hypothalamus (tertiary).
</details>

**Q3.** After a large carbohydrate meal, blood glucose rises. Trace the hormonal response from glucose detection to cellular glucose uptake in muscle.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Elevated blood glucose → pancreatic beta cells detect high glucose → insulin secreted → insulin binds surface receptor (receptor tyrosine kinase) on muscle cells → signaling cascade → GLUT4 vesicles translocate to plasma membrane → GLUT4 inserted → glucose enters muscle cells via facilitated diffusion → blood glucose falls.** Insulin also promotes glycogen synthesis (activates glycogen synthase) and inhibits glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis in the liver. As blood glucose normalizes, insulin secretion decreases (negative feedback via glucose itself).
</details>

**Q4.** A person fasts for 24 hours. Which hormones dominate, and what metabolic processes do they activate?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Glucagon dominates (with cortisol and epinephrine contributing in prolonged fasting).** Glucagon: alpha cells release glucagon → binds liver glucagon receptor (GPCR → cAMP → PKA) → activates glycogenolysis (breaks down glycogen → glucose) and gluconeogenesis (amino acids, glycerol, lactate → glucose) → blood glucose rises. Cortisol (longer fasting): promotes protein catabolism and gluconeogenesis; mobilizes fatty acids. Insulin is suppressed. The brain and RBCs depend on glucose; after glycogen is depleted (~24h), gluconeogenesis and eventually ketogenesis (from fatty acid beta-oxidation) sustain energy.
</details>

**Q5.** A patient with adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) lacks aldosterone. What happens to serum Na⁺, K⁺, and blood pressure?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Serum Na⁺ decreases, K⁺ increases, and blood pressure drops.** Aldosterone normally acts on principal cells of the collecting duct: ↑Na⁺ reabsorption (via ENaC channels) and ↑K⁺ excretion (via ROMK channels). Without aldosterone: Na⁺ is lost in urine (hyponatremia) → water follows → decreased blood volume and hypotension. K⁺ accumulates (hyperkalemia) → risk of fatal cardiac arrhythmia. This is the hallmark triad of Addison's: hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + hypotension. Treatment: fludrocortisone (synthetic mineralocorticoid).
</details>

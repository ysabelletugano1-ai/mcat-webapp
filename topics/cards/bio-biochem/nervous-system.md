# Nervous System & Action Potentials
**AAMC Category:** 3A | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
Neurons communicate via electrical signals (action potentials) generated by sequential ion channel opening, and chemical signals (neurotransmitters) at synapses. The resting membrane potential (~−70 mV) is maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase and K⁺ leak channels. Depolarization above threshold triggers an all-or-nothing action potential that propagates along the axon.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Resting membrane potential**: ~−70 mV (inside negative); maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in) plus K⁺ leak channels
- **Action potential sequence**: Resting (−70 mV) → Depolarization (Na⁺ rushes IN via voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; membrane → +30 mV) → Repolarization (K⁺ rushes OUT via voltage-gated K⁺ channels) → Hyperpolarization (K⁺ channels slow to close; membrane briefly more negative than −70 mV) → Return to resting
- **Threshold**: ~−55 mV; all-or-nothing principle — subthreshold stimulus produces no AP; suprathreshold produces same AP
- **Absolute refractory period**: Na⁺ channels are inactivated (not just closed); no AP possible regardless of stimulus strength; determines maximum firing rate
- **Relative refractory period**: K⁺ channels still partially open (hyperpolarized); a stronger-than-normal stimulus can trigger AP
- **Saltatory conduction**: in myelinated axons, AP "jumps" between nodes of Ranvier; faster and more energy-efficient than continuous conduction in unmyelinated axons
- **Synapse**: presynaptic AP → Ca²⁺ influx (voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels) → vesicle fusion → neurotransmitter release into synaptic cleft → NT binds postsynaptic receptor → EPSP or IPSP
- **Key neurotransmitters**: ACh (NMJ and parasympathetic; also CNS); Dopamine (reward, motor control); Serotonin (mood, sleep); GABA (major inhibitory NT; opens Cl⁻ channels → hyperpolarization); Glutamate (major excitatory NT); Norepinephrine (sympathetic, fight-or-flight)
- **Divisions**: CNS (brain + spinal cord); PNS = Somatic (voluntary) + Autonomic (Sympathetic "fight-or-flight" vs. Parasympathetic "rest-and-digest")

## Mnemonic
**"Depolarization = Na IN, Repolarization = K OUT"** — Na IN starts the wave; K OUT ends it. Think: **Na**tion goes IN(vades), K**nights** go OUT (retreat).

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Jack Westin tutors*
Depolarization = Na⁺ IN. Repolarization = K⁺ OUT. Remember: sodium goes in, potassium goes out — like a revolving door that always goes Na in, K out. And critically: **Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase maintains the resting potential but does NOT generate the action potential.** The AP is generated by voltage-gated ion channels. The ATPase just resets the ion gradients between firings.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase maintains the resting potential but does NOT generate the action potential. The AP is generated by voltage-gated ion channels opening in sequence. Students often say "the pump fires the neuron" — it doesn't. It restores the gradients after firing.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** Arrange the following events of an action potential in correct order: K⁺ channels open, Na⁺ channels inactivate, membrane reaches +30 mV, Na⁺ channels open, K⁺ channels close slowly.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Correct order: Na⁺ channels open → membrane reaches +30 mV → Na⁺ channels inactivate → K⁺ channels open → K⁺ channels close slowly (hyperpolarization).** At threshold, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open rapidly → Na⁺ rushes in → membrane depolarizes to ~+30 mV. Na⁺ channels then inactivate (not just close — they're locked shut). Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open (slower) → K⁺ rushes out → repolarization. K⁺ channels close slowly → hyperpolarization overshoot before returning to −70 mV.
</details>

**Q2.** During the absolute refractory period, a very strong stimulus is applied to the axon. Will an action potential be generated? Why or why not?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**No — no action potential is possible during the absolute refractory period, regardless of stimulus strength.** During this period, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are in the inactivated state — they cannot be reopened by depolarization because they require repolarization to return to the closed (resting) state first. Since the Na⁺ channels cannot open, depolarization above threshold is impossible. This limits the maximum firing frequency of a neuron and ensures AP propagates in one direction only (the region just behind an AP is refractory).
</details>

**Q3.** Multiple sclerosis destroys myelin sheaths. How does demyelination affect action potential conduction, and why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Demyelination slows or blocks conduction because the AP can no longer jump between nodes of Ranvier (saltatory conduction).** In myelinated axons, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are concentrated at nodes; current flows passively under myelin between nodes — fast, efficient. Without myelin, the AP must regenerate at every point along the membrane (continuous conduction), which is much slower and requires more energy. In severe demyelination, the AP may fail to propagate entirely (conduction block), leading to weakness, sensory loss, and coordination deficits.
</details>

**Q4.** Match each neurotransmitter to its primary function/disorder: (A) GABA, (B) Dopamine, (C) Serotonin, (D) Acetylcholine.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**(A) GABA**: major inhibitory neurotransmitter; opens Cl⁻ channels → hyperpolarization; reduced in anxiety; target of benzodiazepines and alcohol. **(B) Dopamine**: reward and motor control; depleted in Parkinson's disease (loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra); excess linked to schizophrenia. **(C) Serotonin**: mood regulation, sleep, appetite; depleted in depression; SSRIs increase synaptic serotonin. **(D) Acetylcholine**: NMJ (voluntary movement); autonomic ganglia; CNS memory; degraded by acetylcholinesterase; blocked by nerve agents; depleted in Alzheimer's disease (basal forebrain).
</details>

**Q5.** What is the role of Ca²⁺ at the presynaptic terminal, and what would happen to neurotransmission if voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels were blocked?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Ca²⁺ influx is the trigger for synaptic vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release.** When an AP arrives at the presynaptic terminal, voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ flows in (extracellular [Ca²⁺] is much higher than intracellular) → Ca²⁺ binds synaptotagmin (a vesicle protein) → vesicles fuse with presynaptic membrane → neurotransmitter released by exocytosis. If Ca²⁺ channels are blocked (e.g., Lambert-Eaton syndrome, certain toxins), vesicle fusion fails → no NT release → no postsynaptic response → neuromuscular or synaptic transmission fails.
</details>

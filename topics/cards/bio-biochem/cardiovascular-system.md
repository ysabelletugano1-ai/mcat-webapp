# Cardiovascular System
**AAMC Category:** 3B | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
The cardiovascular system delivers oxygen and nutrients and removes waste via two circuits: pulmonary (right heart → lungs → left heart) and systemic (left heart → body → right heart). Cardiac output (CO = HR × SV) is regulated by preload, afterload, and contractility. Blood pressure and flow are controlled primarily at the arterioles.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Cardiac output (CO)**: CO = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV); normal ~5 L/min at rest
- **Stroke volume determinants**: Preload (ventricular end-diastolic volume, EDV), Afterload (resistance the ventricle must overcome = peripheral vascular resistance), Contractility (intrinsic force of contraction, affected by sympathetic stimulation and Ca²⁺)
- **Frank-Starling law**: greater preload → greater ventricular stretch → more cross-bridge formation → stronger contraction → greater SV; works up to a limit (over-stretching reduces force)
- **Blood pressure**: systolic (ventricle contracts and ejects blood) / diastolic (ventricle relaxes, filling); normal ~120/80 mmHg; pulse pressure = systolic − diastolic
- **Pulmonary circulation**: right ventricle → pulmonary artery (deoxygenated) → lungs → pulmonary vein (oxygenated) → left atrium; LOW pressure system (~25/8 mmHg)
- **Systemic circulation**: left ventricle → aorta → arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins → vena cava → right atrium; HIGH pressure system
- **Blood flow ∝ 1/resistance**: greatest resistance at arterioles (site of blood pressure regulation via vasoconstriction/dilation); capillaries have greatest total cross-sectional area → slowest flow (allows exchange)
- **Blood composition**: plasma (55% — proteins, water, electrolytes, hormones) + formed elements (45% = RBCs, WBCs, platelets); hematocrit = %RBCs
- **Hemoglobin**: each Hb carries 4 O₂; sigmoidal O₂-dissociation curve due to cooperativity
- **Bohr effect**: ↑CO₂ or ↓pH (acidic, exercising tissue) → Hb releases O₂; ↑O₂ (at lungs) → Hb loads O₂; CO₂ also carried as bicarbonate (70%), dissolved (10%), carbaminohemoglobin (20%)

## Mnemonic
**"CO = HR × SV"** — When one drops, the other must rise to compensate. Think: **C**ars **O**verall = **H**orsepower × **S**peed (rate × volume per beat).

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
CO = HR × SV. If HR doubles and SV halves, CO stays the same. MCAT loves asking about compensation mechanisms — when one variable drops, the body compensates by increasing another. Example: in hemorrhage, SV drops (less preload) → reflex tachycardia (↑HR) to maintain CO. In heart failure, CO drops → sympathetic activation increases HR and contractility as compensation.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Arteries carry oxygenated blood and veins carry deoxygenated blood — EXCEPT in the pulmonary circuit: the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood (from right ventricle to lungs) and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood (from lungs to left atrium). The rule is: arteries carry blood AWAY from the heart; veins carry blood TOWARD the heart — regardless of O₂ content.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A patient's heart rate drops from 70 to 50 bpm due to medication. If cardiac output is maintained, what must have happened to stroke volume?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Stroke volume must have increased from ~71 mL to ~100 mL to maintain the same CO.** CO = HR × SV. If initial CO = 70 × 71 mL = ~5,000 mL/min, and HR drops to 50, then SV must = 5,000/50 = 100 mL. The body can compensate via increased preload (more ventricular filling time at lower HR → greater EDV → Frank-Starling mechanism) and/or increased sympathetic tone increasing contractility.
</details>

**Q2.** During vigorous exercise, skeletal muscle capillaries dilate and CO₂ production rises sharply. Using the Bohr effect, explain what happens to hemoglobin O₂ saturation in the muscle capillaries.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Hb O₂ saturation drops sharply in exercising muscle — Hb releases more O₂ to meet metabolic demand.** The Bohr effect: rising CO₂ lowers pH (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻). Lower pH and higher CO₂ allosterically shift the O₂-dissociation curve rightward — Hb's affinity for O₂ decreases, causing O₂ to be released at the same partial pressure. Additionally, increased temperature and 2,3-BPG in exercising muscle further shift the curve right, maximizing O₂ delivery to working tissue.
</details>

**Q3.** A patient has pulmonary hypertension (elevated pulmonary vascular resistance). Which heart chamber is most directly affected, and how?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The right ventricle (RV) is most directly affected — it must work against elevated afterload.** The RV pumps blood into the pulmonary artery against pulmonary vascular resistance. Normally this is a low-pressure system (~25 mmHg systolic). In pulmonary hypertension, resistance rises → RV must generate more force (increased afterload) → RV hypertrophy over time. If untreated, RV eventually fails (cor pulmonale), reducing CO to the lungs and causing venous congestion (edema, ascites). The LEFT ventricle is not primarily affected because pulmonary hypertension is upstream of it.
</details>

**Q4.** Why does blood flow slowest through capillaries, even though their individual diameters are the smallest of any vessel?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Because capillaries have the greatest total cross-sectional area of any vessel type.** Flow velocity = Flow rate / Cross-sectional area. Although individual capillaries are narrow, there are billions of them in parallel — their combined cross-sectional area far exceeds that of the aorta. By the continuity equation, the same volume of blood must pass through a larger total area → velocity drops. Slow flow in capillaries is functionally essential: it maximizes time for O₂, CO₂, nutrients, and waste to exchange between blood and tissues.
</details>

**Q5.** According to Frank-Starling law, what happens to stroke volume when a patient loses 1 liter of blood rapidly? What is the compensatory mechanism?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Stroke volume decreases because preload (EDV) drops — less blood returns to the heart (reduced venous return).** With less ventricular filling, the ventricle is stretched less → cross-bridge formation is reduced → weaker contraction → lower SV → lower CO → hypotension. Compensatory mechanisms: (1) Sympathetic activation → tachycardia (↑HR) and vasoconstriction (↑afterload shifts blood centrally) and ↑contractility; (2) RAAS activation → aldosterone → Na⁺ and water retention → restores blood volume; (3) ADH release → water reabsorption → plasma volume restoration. These responses try to restore CO toward baseline.
</details>

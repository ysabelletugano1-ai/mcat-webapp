# DNA Replication & Repair
**AAMC Category:** 1B | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
DNA replication is semiconservative (each new double helix contains one original strand), proceeds 5'→3', and requires a primer. The leading strand is synthesized continuously; the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments. Key enzymes have distinct, frequently tested roles.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Helicase**: unwinds double helix at replication fork
- **Primase**: synthesizes short RNA primer (DNA pol cannot start from scratch)
- **DNA Pol III**: main synthesizing enzyme; adds nucleotides 5'→3'; proofreads 3'→5' exonuclease activity
- **DNA Pol I**: removes RNA primer; replaces with DNA
- **Ligase**: seals nicks between Okazaki fragments
- **SSBPs**: single-strand binding proteins stabilize unwound DNA
- **Topoisomerase**: relieves supercoiling ahead of fork (target of fluoroquinolone antibiotics)
- **Leading strand**: continuous, synthesized toward replication fork
- **Lagging strand**: discontinuous (Okazaki fragments), synthesized away from fork
- **Semiconservative replication**: proven by Meselson-Stahl experiment (¹⁵N/¹⁴N density gradient)
- **Telomerase**: adds TTAGGG repeats to chromosome ends; active in stem cells and cancer; absent in somatic cells → telomere shortening with age

## Mnemonic
**"Helicase Pulls, Primase Primes, Pol III Builds, Pol I Removes, Ligase Links"**

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT Biology guide*
Direction shortcut: **Template strand is read 3'→5'. New strand is built 5'→3'. Always.** Leading strand goes toward the fork (one continuous piece). Lagging strand goes away from the fork (multiple Okazaki fragments, each needing its own primer). When a question shows a replication fork diagram, identify which strand runs 3'→5' toward the fork — that's the template for the leading strand.

## Common Trap
⚠️ DNA polymerase III cannot initiate synthesis — it needs a pre-existing 3'-OH to extend. That's what the RNA primer from primase provides. This is why RNA primers are necessary even though RNA is ultimately replaced by DNA.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** During DNA replication, which enzyme is responsible for replacing the RNA primer with DNA?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**DNA polymerase I.** DNA Pol I has 5'→3' exonuclease activity that removes the RNA primer while simultaneously synthesizing DNA in the gap. DNA Pol III cannot remove the primer — it only extends from an existing 3'-OH. Ligase then seals the remaining nick.
</details>

**Q2.** A mutation inactivates topoisomerase II in a replicating cell. What is the most likely consequence?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Replication fork stalls due to supercoiling.** As helicase unwinds the double helix, positive supercoils accumulate ahead of the fork. Topoisomerase II (and Topoisomerase I) relieve this torsional stress by cutting, rotating, and re-ligating DNA strands. Without it, the helix becomes too tightly wound ahead of the fork to continue unwinding.
</details>

**Q3.** The Meselson-Stahl experiment grew bacteria in ¹⁵N medium, then transferred to ¹⁴N. After one generation, all DNA was intermediate density. After two generations, half was intermediate and half was light. What does this support?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Semiconservative replication.** Each new double helix contains one parental strand (heavy) and one new strand (light) → intermediate density after generation 1. After generation 2, each intermediate molecule produces one intermediate and one light molecule → 50:50 ratio. Conservative replication would predict all-heavy + all-light after generation 1 (not seen).
</details>

**Q4.** Cancer cells overexpress telomerase. Why does telomerase activity confer a proliferative advantage?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Telomerase prevents telomere shortening, enabling unlimited replication.** Normal somatic cells lose telomere length with each division (DNA polymerase cannot replicate the lagging strand end). After enough shortening, the cell enters senescence or apoptosis. Cancer cells reactivate telomerase, adding TTAGGG repeats back, preventing this limit and allowing indefinite division.
</details>

**Q5.** Which strand of DNA serves as the template for the leading strand, and in which direction is the leading strand synthesized relative to the replication fork?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The template for the leading strand runs 3'→5' in the direction of fork movement.** The leading strand is synthesized 5'→3' toward (and in the same direction as) the moving replication fork, enabling continuous synthesis. The lagging strand template runs the opposite direction, forcing discontinuous synthesis away from the fork.
</details>

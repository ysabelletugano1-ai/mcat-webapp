# Cell Membrane & Transport
**AAMC Category:** 2A | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
The cell membrane is a fluid mosaic of phospholipids and proteins. Its selective permeability controls which substances enter or leave the cell. Transport can be passive (down concentration/electrochemical gradient, no ATP) or active (against gradient, requires ATP or cotransport).

## High-Yield Facts
- **Phospholipid bilayer**: hydrophilic heads face aqueous environments; hydrophobic tails face inward
- **Membrane fluidity**: increased by unsaturated fatty acids (kinks prevent tight packing) and cholesterol (moderates fluidity — increases fluidity at low temps, decreases at high temps)
- **Simple diffusion**: small, nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂, N₂, ethanol); no protein needed
- **Facilitated diffusion**: polar/charged molecules; uses channel or carrier proteins; passive (down gradient); no ATP
- **Primary active transport**: directly uses ATP (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP)
- **Secondary active transport**: uses Na⁺ electrochemical gradient established by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase; symport (same direction) or antiport (opposite direction)
- **Osmosis**: water moves toward higher solute concentration (lower water potential)
- **Endocytosis/exocytosis**: bulk transport; requires energy; vesicle-mediated
- **Membrane potential**: inside of cell is negative relative to outside (~−70 mV at rest)

## Mnemonic
**"Na⁺ goes OUT, K⁺ goes IN — 3 out, 2 in, costs 1 ATP"** (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase)

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
Secondary active transport shortcut: **It's still "active" even though it doesn't directly use ATP.** The Na⁺ gradient IS the energy source (indirectly powered by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase using ATP elsewhere). MCAT loves asking whether secondary active transport requires ATP — technically no (the transporter itself doesn't hydrolyze ATP), but the system depends on ATP-driven Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to maintain the gradient.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Cholesterol increases membrane fluidity at low temperatures but decreases it at high temperatures. It acts as a buffer/moderator. Students often only know one direction.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A cell is placed in a hypertonic solution. In which direction will water move, and what happens to cell volume?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Water moves OUT of the cell; cell shrinks (crenation in RBCs).** Hypertonic = higher solute concentration outside than inside. Water moves by osmosis from low solute (cell interior) to high solute (extracellular fluid), following its concentration gradient. The cell loses water and shrinks. In hypotonic solution, the opposite occurs — water enters, cell swells (may lyse).
</details>

**Q2.** Glucose enters most cells via GLUT transporters (facilitated diffusion). If intracellular glucose is phosphorylated to glucose-6-phosphate immediately after entry, how does this affect glucose transport into the cell?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**It maintains the inward glucose concentration gradient, driving continued uptake.** G6P cannot exit through GLUT transporters (it's charged and GLUT is specific for glucose). By phosphorylating glucose, the cell keeps intracellular free glucose low, maintaining the gradient from high (extracellular) to low (intracellular). This is how cells "trap" glucose — not active transport, but smart use of facilitated diffusion plus immediate metabolism.
</details>

**Q3.** A drug blocks the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. What is the immediate consequence for secondary active transport of glucose in intestinal epithelial cells?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Glucose absorption via SGLT (Na⁺-glucose symporter) stops.** SGLT uses the inward Na⁺ gradient to cotransport glucose against its own concentration gradient into the cell. This gradient is maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. If the ATPase is blocked, the Na⁺ gradient dissipates, and SGLT loses its driving force. Glucose can no longer be actively absorbed even though SGLT itself is unaffected.
</details>

**Q4.** Which membrane component is most responsible for making the plasma membrane impermeable to ions?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The hydrophobic phospholipid bilayer core.** Ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻) are charged and cannot cross the hydrophobic interior of the bilayer. They require specific channel or transporter proteins. Small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) can cross freely; ions cannot without protein assistance.
</details>

**Q5.** Endocytosis packages extracellular material into vesicles. Which type of endocytosis specifically involves receptor-mediated internalization of specific ligands?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Receptor-mediated endocytosis (clathrin-coated pit pathway).** Specific receptors on the cell surface bind their ligands (e.g., LDL receptor binds LDL cholesterol). The receptor-ligand complex is internalized via clathrin-coated vesicles. Compare: phagocytosis (large particles, used by immune cells), pinocytosis (fluid and solutes, nonspecific), and receptor-mediated (specific, ligand-dependent).
</details>

# Enzyme Kinetics & Inhibition
**AAMC Category:** 1A — Structure and function of proteins and amino acids
**Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

---

## Core Concept
Enzymes lower activation energy without being consumed. Michaelis-Menten kinetics describes how reaction velocity relates to substrate concentration, with Vmax (maximum velocity) and Km (substrate concentration at half-Vmax) as the two key parameters. Inhibitors alter these parameters in predictable ways that the MCAT tests directly with Lineweaver-Burk plots.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Km**: substrate concentration at ½ Vmax; low Km = high affinity for substrate
- **Vmax**: maximum reaction rate at saturating substrate; proportional to enzyme concentration
- **Competitive inhibition**: inhibitor binds active site; Km↑, Vmax unchanged; overcome by excess substrate
- **Noncompetitive inhibition**: inhibitor binds allosteric site; Vmax↓, Km unchanged; cannot overcome with substrate
- **Uncompetitive inhibition**: inhibitor binds only enzyme-substrate complex; both Km and Vmax decrease proportionally
- **Mixed inhibition**: inhibitor can bind free enzyme or ES complex; both Km and Vmax change, but not proportionally
- **Irreversible inhibition**: covalently modifies enzyme (e.g., aspirin, nerve agents); permanent reduction in activity
- **Allosteric regulation**: feedback inhibition by end products; sigmoidal (not hyperbolic) velocity curves
- **Zymogen**: inactive enzyme precursor activated by cleavage (pepsinogen → pepsin)

## Mnemonic
**"CAN'T Undo Mistakes"** — Competitive (Km↑), Allosteric/Noncompetitive (Vmax↓), Uncompetitive (both↓), Mixed (both change)

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Tutor-verified (Blueprint MCAT + r/MCAT 520+ threads):*
**Lineweaver-Burk (double-reciprocal) plot interpretation:**
- Lines intersect **on the Y-axis** → Competitive (same Vmax = same y-intercept, different Km = different x-intercept)
- Lines intersect **on the X-axis** → Noncompetitive (same Km = same x-intercept, different Vmax = different y-intercept)
- Lines are **parallel** → Uncompetitive (both Km and Vmax change by same factor → same slope, parallel lines)
You don't need to memorize the math. Just know where the lines intersect.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Noncompetitive inhibitors cannot be overcome by adding more substrate — this is the most common wrong answer on enzyme inhibition questions. Only competitive inhibitors are overcome by excess substrate.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A researcher adds an inhibitor to an enzyme reaction and observes that Km increases but Vmax remains unchanged. Adding excess substrate restores the original reaction rate. Which type of inhibitor is this?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Competitive inhibitor.** Km increases because the inhibitor competes with substrate for the active site, requiring more substrate to reach half-maximal velocity. Since the inhibitor and substrate compete, flooding with substrate displaces the inhibitor, restoring Vmax. Noncompetitive inhibitors reduce Vmax and cannot be overcome with more substrate.
</details>

**Q2.** On a Lineweaver-Burk plot, two lines (uninhibited and inhibited) intersect exactly on the x-axis. Which inhibition type does this represent, and what does the x-intercept represent?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Noncompetitive inhibition.** The x-axis intersection means the x-intercept (−1/Km) is unchanged — Km is the same with and without inhibitor. The y-intercept (1/Vmax) differs, indicating Vmax is reduced. The x-intercept = −1/Km.
</details>

**Q3.** An enzyme has a Km of 2 mM. At what substrate concentration does the enzyme operate at 50% of Vmax?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**2 mM.** By definition, Km is the substrate concentration at which reaction velocity equals exactly ½ Vmax. No calculation needed — Km is defined this way.
</details>

**Q4.** A drug irreversibly inhibits an enzyme by covalently modifying its active site serine residue. A researcher doubles the enzyme concentration in the reaction. What happens to Vmax?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Vmax increases proportionally (doubles).** Vmax is proportional to total enzyme concentration ([E]total). Even though a fraction of enzymes are irreversibly inhibited, adding more enzyme increases the pool of uninhibited enzyme. The remaining uninhibited enzymes can still reach Vmax. (Compare: reversible inhibitors don't benefit from adding enzyme — they benefit only from changing substrate/inhibitor concentrations.)
</details>

**Q5.** Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1) is allosterically inhibited by ATP and activated by AMP. A cell is starved of nutrients. What happens to PFK-1 activity, and what is the physiological consequence?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**PFK-1 activity increases.** Nutrient starvation → low ATP, high AMP → AMP activates PFK-1 → glycolysis accelerates → more ATP produced. This is negative feedback regulation: low energy state signals the cell to make more energy. PFK-1 is the rate-limiting step of glycolysis; its allosteric regulation controls the entire pathway's flux.
</details>

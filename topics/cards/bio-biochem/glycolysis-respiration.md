# Glycolysis & Cellular Respiration
**AAMC Category:** 1D | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
Glucose is oxidized in a stepwise sequence to produce ATP: glycolysis (cytoplasm) → pyruvate decarboxylation → Krebs cycle (mitochondrial matrix) → oxidative phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane). The electron carriers NADH and FADH₂ shuttle electrons to the ETC.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Glycolysis net**: 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvate per glucose (in cytoplasm; no O₂ required)
- **Pyruvate decarboxylation**: pyruvate → acetyl-CoA; produces 1 NADH, 1 CO₂ per pyruvate (×2 = 2 NADH, 2 CO₂)
- **Krebs cycle** (×2 per glucose): 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 ATP (GTP), 4 CO₂ total
- **ETC**: NADH → ~2.5 ATP; FADH₂ → ~1.5 ATP; total ~32 ATP from ETC
- **Total net yield**: ~36–38 ATP per glucose (textbook); ~30–32 ATP (physiologically)
- **Rate-limiting step of glycolysis**: PFK-1; inhibited by ATP and citrate; activated by AMP
- **Hexokinase**: first step; inhibited by G6P (product inhibition)
- **Pyruvate kinase**: last step of glycolysis
- **Fermentation**: regenerates NAD⁺ when O₂ absent; lactate (animals) or ethanol + CO₂ (yeast)
- **Gluconeogenesis**: NOT simply reverse glycolysis; 3 irreversible steps bypassed by unique enzymes; occurs in liver/kidney

## Mnemonic
**"Pay 2, Get 4, Net 2"** — glycolysis invests 2 ATP upfront, produces 4, net 2 ATP per glucose

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: MedSchoolCoach MCAT tutors*
**Krebs ATP accounting shortcut**: Per turn: 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP. Runs TWICE per glucose → ×2. Don't add them up in your head — just know the per-turn outputs and multiply. The MCAT usually asks about per-turn outputs or asks you to identify where CO₂ is released (pyruvate decarboxylation and Krebs, not glycolysis or ETC).

## Common Trap
⚠️ CO₂ is NOT produced in glycolysis or the ETC — only in pyruvate decarboxylation and the Krebs cycle. Students frequently place CO₂ production at glycolysis when asked where carbon leaves as CO₂.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A cell is treated with a drug that blocks Complex I of the electron transport chain. Which electron carrier will accumulate?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**NADH.** Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) is the entry point for electrons from NADH. If blocked, NADH cannot be oxidized to NAD⁺, so NADH accumulates. This also backs up the Krebs cycle (which requires NAD⁺) and ultimately slows ATP synthesis. FADH₂ enters at Complex II and would not directly accumulate if only Complex I is blocked.
</details>

**Q2.** Under anaerobic conditions, yeast converts pyruvate to ethanol and CO₂. What is the primary purpose of this fermentation reaction?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**To regenerate NAD⁺** so glycolysis can continue. Glycolysis produces NADH from NAD⁺. If NAD⁺ is not regenerated, glycolysis stops (no oxidizing agent for G3P dehydrogenase). Fermentation oxidizes NADH back to NAD⁺, allowing continued ATP production from glycolysis even without oxygen.
</details>

**Q3.** PFK-1 is allosterically inhibited by high concentrations of ATP and citrate. What is the physiological significance of citrate inhibition?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Citrate signals that the Krebs cycle is already well-supplied, so glycolysis should slow.** When the Krebs cycle is running efficiently, citrate (the first Krebs intermediate) builds up and diffuses into the cytoplasm, inhibiting PFK-1. This negative feedback prevents excess pyruvate from being produced when energy is abundant — a classic example of end-product feedback inhibition.
</details>

**Q4.** Gluconeogenesis produces glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors. Which of the following is NOT a direct precursor for gluconeogenesis?
A) Lactate
B) Alanine
C) Acetyl-CoA
D) Oxaloacetate
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**C — Acetyl-CoA.** Acetyl-CoA cannot be converted back to pyruvate (pyruvate decarboxylation is irreversible) and cannot enter the gluconeogenic pathway directly. Lactate → pyruvate → gluconeogenesis. Alanine → pyruvate (transamination) → gluconeogenesis. Oxaloacetate is a direct intermediate in gluconeogenesis. This is why fat (β-oxidation produces acetyl-CoA) cannot be converted to net glucose in mammals.
</details>

**Q5.** How many net ATP molecules are produced from the complete oxidation of one molecule of pyruvate through the Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**~12.5 ATP.** Pyruvate decarboxylation: 1 NADH (= ~2.5 ATP) + 1 CO₂. Krebs cycle (one turn for one pyruvate): 3 NADH (= ~7.5 ATP) + 1 FADH₂ (= ~1.5 ATP) + 1 GTP (= 1 ATP) + 2 CO₂. Total: 2.5 + 7.5 + 1.5 + 1 = ~12.5 ATP per pyruvate. (For whole glucose, multiply ×2 = ~25 from pyruvate onward, plus the 2 net from glycolysis = ~27–30 total.)
</details>

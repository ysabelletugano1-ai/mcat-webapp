# Cell Cycle, Mitosis & Meiosis
**AAMC Category:** 2C | **Section:** Bio/Biochem | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-bio-biochem-hub]]

## Core Concept
The cell cycle (G1 → S → G2 → M → Cytokinesis) is tightly regulated by cyclins and CDKs at key checkpoints. Mitosis produces 2 identical diploid daughter cells for growth and repair, while meiosis produces 4 genetically unique haploid cells for sexual reproduction. Checkpoint failures drive cancer development.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Cell cycle order**: G1 (growth, protein synthesis) → S (DNA replication) → G2 (prep for division) → M (mitosis: PMAT) → Cytokinesis
- **G1 checkpoint (restriction point)**: regulated by Rb (tumor suppressor) and p53; cell commits to division here; checks for DNA damage and sufficient size
- **G2/M checkpoint**: regulated by cyclin B–CDK1 complex; ensures DNA replication is complete before mitosis
- **Cyclins**: rise and fall throughout the cycle; CDKs are constitutively expressed but inactive alone
- **CDK activation**: cyclin binds CDK → conformational change → active kinase → phosphorylates target proteins → checkpoint passes
- **Mitosis phases — PMAT**: Prophase (chromosomes condense, spindle forms), Metaphase (chromosomes align at metaphase plate), Anaphase (sister chromatids separate, pulled to poles), Telophase (nuclear envelope reforms, chromosomes decondense)
- **Meiosis I (reductive division)**: homologous chromosomes separate → 2 haploid cells (each with replicated chromosomes)
- **Meiosis II**: sister chromatids separate (like mitosis) → 4 haploid cells total
- **Crossing over**: occurs in Prophase I between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes; increases genetic diversity
- **Independent assortment**: random orientation of homolog pairs at Metaphase I → 2²³ possible combinations in humans
- **Cancer**: loss of tumor suppressors (p53, Rb) or gain-of-function mutations in proto-oncogenes (→ oncogenes) → uncontrolled proliferation

## Mnemonic
**"PMAT"** — **P**rophase, **M**etaphase, **A**naphase, **T**elophase (same order in both mitosis and meiosis II)

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Khan Academy MCAT*
Cyclins cycle; CDKs are constant. When cyclin is absent, CDK is inactive. When cyclin rises, CDK is activated → checkpoint passes. Think of CDK as a car engine that only runs when cyclin (the key) is inserted. When cyclin is degraded (key removed), the engine stops — checkpoint engaged again.

## Common Trap
⚠️ Anaphase I = homologs separate (still joined sister chromatids move together). Anaphase II = sister chromatids separate. Many students confuse these. Remember: Meiosis I is the REDUCTIVE division (2n → n); Meiosis II is the SEPARATION division (n → n, splitting sister chromatids).

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A cell has elevated levels of cyclin B. What is the most likely consequence, and at which cell cycle transition does this matter?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Elevated cyclin B activates CDK1, driving the cell past the G2/M checkpoint into mitosis.** Cyclin B–CDK1 (also called MPF, maturation-promoting factor) phosphorylates proteins that trigger nuclear envelope breakdown and spindle assembly. If cyclin B fails to be degraded after mitosis begins, the cell cannot exit mitosis — the cell becomes stuck in M phase. This is the checkpoint that ensures DNA replication is complete before the cell commits to dividing.
</details>

**Q2.** A patient has a mutation inactivating both copies of the Rb gene. What is the most direct consequence for the cell cycle?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The G1 checkpoint (restriction point) is bypassed, and the cell proliferates uncontrollably.** Rb normally binds and inactivates E2F transcription factors (which drive S-phase gene expression). When Rb is phosphorylated by cyclin D–CDK4/6, it releases E2F → cell enters S phase. With Rb absent, E2F is constitutively active → cells continuously enter S phase regardless of growth signals. Rb loss is found in retinoblastoma and many cancers.
</details>

**Q3.** How many chromosomes does a human cell contain at the end of Meiosis I, and how does this differ from a cell at the end of Meiosis II?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**After Meiosis I: 23 chromosomes, each consisting of 2 sister chromatids (still joined at centromere) = 46 chromatids total. After Meiosis II: 23 chromosomes, each a single chromatid = 23 chromatids total.** Meiosis I separates homologs (diploid → haploid in chromosome number but chromatids are still paired). Meiosis II separates sister chromatids, producing cells with 23 single-stranded chromosomes — the mature gametes.
</details>

**Q4.** Why does crossing over in Prophase I increase genetic diversity beyond what independent assortment alone provides?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Crossing over creates new combinations of alleles within individual chromosomes (recombinant chromosomes), whereas independent assortment only shuffles whole chromosomes.** Without crossing over, each chromosome passed to offspring would be entirely maternal or paternal in origin. With crossing over, sections of homologous chromosomes are exchanged, generating chromosomes with novel allele combinations that neither parent possessed. This dramatically expands the genetic variation possible in offspring beyond the 2²³ combinations from assortment alone.
</details>

**Q5.** A cell with a p53 mutation suffers double-stranded DNA breaks after radiation exposure. What happens to this cell compared to a cell with functional p53?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Without functional p53, the cell fails to arrest at the G1 checkpoint and cannot initiate apoptosis — it continues dividing with damaged DNA, accumulating mutations.** Normal p53 detects DNA damage → upregulates p21 (CDK inhibitor) → cell cycle arrest → DNA repair attempted → if unrepairable, p53 triggers apoptosis. Mutant p53 loses this function: the cell bypasses arrest, replicates damaged DNA, passes mutations to daughter cells. p53 is the most commonly mutated gene in human cancers ("guardian of the genome").
</details>

# Acid-Base Chemistry & Buffers
**AAMC Category:** 5A — Unique nature of water and its solutions | **Section:** Chem/Physics | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-chem-physics-hub]]

## Core Concept
Acids donate protons (H⁺); bases accept them (Brønsted-Lowry). Acid-base equilibria are described by Ka and pKa. Buffers resist pH changes by containing a weak acid and its conjugate base. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation predicts pH from concentrations. The MCAT tests buffer calculations, physiological pH, and titration curve interpretation.

## High-Yield Facts
- **Strong acids** (HCl, HBr, HI, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HClO₄): fully dissociate; [H⁺] = [acid]
- **Weak acids**: partial dissociation; described by Ka; lower Ka = weaker acid
- **pKa = −log(Ka)**; lower pKa = stronger acid
- **Henderson-Hasselbalch**: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
- When [A⁻] = [HA]: pH = pKa (half-equivalence point on titration curve)
- **Buffer range**: pKa ± 1 (effective buffering window)
- **Physiological buffers**: bicarbonate (plasma, pKa 6.1), phosphate (intracellular, pKa 7.2), protein (hemoglobin)
- **Titration curve**: S-shaped; flat regions = buffer zones; steep midpoint = equivalence point
- At equivalence point: all weak acid converted to conjugate base; pH > 7 for weak acid/strong base titration
- **Amphoteric species**: can act as acid or base (water, amino acids at intermediate pH)
- **pH scale**: 0–14; pH 7.4 = physiological; each unit = 10× difference in [H⁺]

## Mnemonic
**"pH = pKa when it's 50/50"** — equal concentrations of acid and conjugate base → pH equals pKa exactly

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Jack Westin tutors + Blueprint MCAT*
Henderson-Hasselbalch shortcuts:
- pH > pKa → more base form ([A⁻] > [HA])
- pH < pKa → more acid form ([HA] > [A⁻])
- pH = pKa → 50/50 (maximum buffering capacity)
You rarely need to calculate — just know which form predominates. Amino acid ionization questions use this principle directly: if pH is above the pKa of a group, that group is deprotonated (base form).

## Common Trap
⚠️ At the equivalence point of a weak acid/strong base titration, the solution is BASIC (not neutral). The conjugate base formed (A⁻) hydrolyzes water: A⁻ + H₂O → HA + OH⁻. pH > 7 at equivalence point for any weak acid titration.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A buffer contains 0.1 M acetic acid (pKa = 4.75) and 0.1 M sodium acetate. What is the pH of this buffer?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**pH = 4.75.** When [A⁻] = [HA], the log term in Henderson-Hasselbalch equals log(1) = 0. pH = pKa + 0 = 4.75. No calculation needed beyond recognizing equal concentrations.
</details>

**Q2.** Bicarbonate buffer in blood has pKa = 6.1. At physiological pH of 7.4, which form predominates — H₂CO₃ or HCO₃⁻?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate, the base form) predominates.** pH (7.4) > pKa (6.1), so the equation shifts toward the base form. The ratio [HCO₃⁻]/[H₂CO₃] = 10^(7.4−6.1) = 10^1.3 ≈ 20:1. The base form dominates by ~20-fold at physiological pH.
</details>

**Q3.** A patient hyperventilates, causing CO₂ to drop. Using the bicarbonate buffer equation (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻), predict the effect on blood pH.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Blood pH rises (respiratory alkalosis).** Loss of CO₂ shifts the equilibrium left, reducing H₂CO₃ and thus [H⁺]. Lower [H⁺] = higher pH. This is respiratory alkalosis. Compensation: kidneys excrete HCO₃⁻ to bring pH back toward normal (metabolic compensation).
</details>

**Q4.** On a titration curve for a weak acid (pKa = 5.0) with a strong base, at what volume of base does maximum buffering capacity occur?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**At the half-equivalence point** — where exactly half the acid has been neutralized. At this point, [HA] = [A⁻], pH = pKa = 5.0, and the buffer can absorb equal amounts of added acid or base. On the titration curve, this is the midpoint of the flat (buffering) region, at half the volume of base needed to reach the equivalence point.
</details>

**Q5.** Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, pKa = 3.5) is taken orally. In the stomach (pH 2.0), which form predominates, and how does this affect absorption?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The protonated/acid form (HA) predominates at pH 2.0** (below pKa of 3.5). The acid form is uncharged and lipid-soluble, allowing it to cross cell membranes readily. This is why aspirin is absorbed in the stomach despite the acidic environment — the low pH actually promotes absorption of weak acids by keeping them in the uncharged (membrane-permeable) form. This is the "ion trapping" principle.
</details>

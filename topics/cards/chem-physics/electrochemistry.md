# Electrochemistry & Electrical Circuits
**AAMC Category:** 4C — Electrochemistry and electrical circuits | **Section:** Chem/Physics | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-chem-physics-hub]]

## Core Concept
Electrochemistry links redox reactions to electrical work. The cell potential (E°cell) determines spontaneity: positive E°cell means the reaction is spontaneous. The relationships ΔG° = −nFE°cell and the Nernst equation connect thermodynamics to electrochemistry. The MCAT tests galvanic vs. electrolytic cells, reduction potential tables, and biological redox (NADH, FADH₂ as electron carriers).

## High-Yield Facts
- **E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode**; positive E°cell = spontaneous reaction
- **ΔG° = −nFE°cell** (n = moles of electrons transferred; F = Faraday's constant, 96,485 C/mol)
- Positive E°cell → negative ΔG° → spontaneous (consistent with thermodynamics)
- **Nernst equation**: E = E° − (RT/nF)lnQ; at 25°C simplifies to E = E° − (0.0592/n)logQ
- **Galvanic (voltaic) cell**: spontaneous redox reaction generates electrical current; ΔG < 0
- **Electrolytic cell**: external electrical energy forces a non-spontaneous reaction; ΔG > 0
- **Cathode** = site of reduction (gain of electrons) in BOTH cell types
- **Anode** = site of oxidation (loss of electrons) in BOTH cell types
- In galvanic cells: anode is negative (−), cathode is positive (+)
- In electrolytic cells: anode is positive (+), cathode is negative (−) — REVERSED sign convention
- **Higher reduction potential** = stronger oxidizing agent (more likely to be reduced)
- **Lower reduction potential** = stronger reducing agent (more likely to be oxidized)
- **Biological redox**: NAD⁺/NADH (E° = −0.32 V); FAD/FADH₂; O₂/H₂O (E° = +0.82 V) — electrons flow from NADH to O₂ in the ETC
- **Salt bridge**: maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ion flow between half-cells

## Mnemonic
**"LEO GER"** — Loss of Electrons = Oxidation; Gain of Electrons = Reduction. Plus: **"An Ox, Red Cat"** — Anode = Oxidation; Reduction = Cathode.

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Blueprint MCAT*
Three-step approach to any electrochemistry problem:
1. Identify cathode (higher E°reduction) and anode (lower E°reduction) from the table
2. Calculate E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (if positive → spontaneous)
3. Use ΔG° = −nFE°cell to get thermodynamic favorability

For Nernst: if Q > 1 (products accumulate), E decreases below E° (cell runs down). If Q < 1 (reactants dominate), E > E° (cell runs above standard). At equilibrium, E = 0 and Q = Keq.

## Common Trap
⚠️ In electrolytic cells, the cathode and anode ELECTRODE SIGNS are reversed from galvanic cells. In an electrolytic cell, the cathode is connected to the negative terminal of the power source (where electrons are pumped in to drive reduction). Students who memorize "cathode is positive" from galvanic cells get this wrong. The electrochemical definitions (cathode = reduction, anode = oxidation) never change — only the electrode polarities differ.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** Given: Cu²⁺/Cu E° = +0.34 V; Zn²⁺/Zn E° = −0.76 V. Calculate E°cell for a galvanic cell using these half-reactions.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**E°cell = +1.10 V (spontaneous).** Copper has the higher reduction potential, so Cu²⁺ is reduced at the cathode. Zinc is oxidized at the anode. E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = (+0.34) − (−0.76) = +1.10 V. This is a classic Daniel cell. ΔG° = −nFE° = −(2)(96,485)(1.10) ≈ −212 kJ/mol.
</details>

**Q2.** Two half-reactions have E° values of +0.80 V and +0.34 V. Which species acts as the oxidizing agent in a spontaneous cell?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**The species with E° = +0.80 V is the oxidizing agent.** Higher reduction potential means stronger tendency to be reduced — i.e., it is the better oxidizing agent. It becomes the cathode (is reduced). The species with lower E° (+0.34 V) is oxidized at the anode and acts as the reducing agent.
</details>

**Q3.** In an electrolytic cell used for electroplating copper, at which electrode does copper metal deposit, and what is the sign of that electrode?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Copper deposits at the cathode, which carries a negative sign in an electrolytic cell.** Cu²⁺ ions are reduced (gain electrons) to form Cu metal: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu. Reduction always occurs at the cathode. In an electrolytic cell, the power source pushes electrons into the cathode, making it the negative electrode. The object being plated is connected as the cathode.
</details>

**Q4.** A galvanic cell has E°cell = +0.50 V. The reaction quotient Q is greater than Keq. What can you predict about the actual cell potential E and the direction of spontaneity?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**E < 0; the reaction is no longer spontaneous in the forward direction.** When Q > Keq, the system has overshot equilibrium — products have accumulated beyond the equilibrium ratio. The Nernst equation gives E = E° − (0.0592/n)logQ. Since Q > Keq and E° = 0 at equilibrium (by definition when Q = Keq), Q > Keq forces E < 0. The cell would actually run in reverse if it could. In practice, the cell has been discharged past equilibrium.
</details>

**Q5.** NADH has a reduction potential of −0.32 V and O₂/H₂O has a reduction potential of +0.82 V in the electron transport chain. Calculate E°cell and explain why this drives ATP synthesis.
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**E°cell = +1.14 V; ΔG° ≈ −220 kJ/mol (highly favorable).** NADH is oxidized at the "anode" (E° = −0.32 V); O₂ is reduced at the "cathode" (E° = +0.82 V). E°cell = 0.82 − (−0.32) = +1.14 V. With n = 2 electrons per NADH: ΔG° = −(2)(96,485)(1.14) ≈ −220 kJ/mol. This large release of free energy drives proton pumping across the inner mitochondrial membrane, creating the proton gradient used by ATP synthase to synthesize ~2.5 ATP per NADH.
</details>

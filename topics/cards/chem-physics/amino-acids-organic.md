# Amino Acids & Organic Chemistry of Biomolecules
**AAMC Category:** 5D — Structure, function, and reactivity of biologically relevant molecules | **Section:** Chem/Physics | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-chem-physics-hub]]

## Core Concept
Amino acids are the building blocks of proteins, each defined by a unique R group that determines charge, polarity, and reactivity. At physiological pH, amino acids exist as zwitterions (simultaneously carrying + and − charges). The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which an amino acid has zero net charge. Understanding amino acid charge at different pH values is essential for protein structure, enzyme function, gel electrophoresis, and MCAT passage interpretation.

## High-Yield Facts
- **20 standard amino acids**: all have a central α-carbon, amino group (−NH₂), carboxyl group (−COOH), hydrogen, and a unique R group
- **Zwitterion**: at physiological pH (~7.4), the α-amino group is protonated (−NH₃⁺) and the α-carboxyl is deprotonated (−COO⁻) — net charge depends on R group
- **pI (isoelectric point)**: pH at which net charge = 0; amino acid does not migrate in electric field
- **Charge rule**: pH > pI → net negative charge; pH < pI → net positive charge; pH = pI → net zero
- **Acidic amino acids** (Asp, Glu): negatively charged R group at pH 7; pI < 7 (~3)
- **Basic amino acids** (Lys, Arg, His): positively charged R group at pH 7; pI > 7 (~8–10)
- **Nonpolar hydrophobic**: Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Met, Phe, Trp, Pro — buried in protein core
- **Polar uncharged**: Ser, Thr, Cys, Tyr, Asn, Gln — R groups can H-bond
- **Cys**: unique — thiol (−SH) group forms disulfide bonds (−S−S−) under oxidizing conditions; broken by reducing agents (β-mercaptoethanol, DTT)
- **Pro (proline)**: R group bonds back to α-nitrogen → rigid cyclic structure → disrupts α-helices and β-sheets; creates "kinks" in secondary structure
- **Gly (glycine)**: smallest amino acid; no chiral center (R group = H); most conformational flexibility; found at bends/turns
- **His**: pKa ~6 for imidazole R group — the only amino acid that changes protonation near physiological pH; critical for enzyme active site catalysis (acid-base catalyst)
- **Electrophoresis**: at pH above pI, amino acid is negatively charged → migrates toward anode (+); below pI → positively charged → migrates toward cathode (−)
- **pI calculation**: for simple amino acids, pI = average of the two pKa values flanking the neutral form

## Mnemonic
**"DEAD" for acidic** — Asp and Glu Are Acidic (negatively charged at pH 7). **"KRHQ" for basic** — Lys (K), Arg (R), His (H) are positively charged at pH 7. "At pH above pI: Anions (negative); Below pI: Basic (positive) — ABBA rule."

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Jack Westin tutors*
For any amino acid charge question:
1. Find pI (given or calculate as average of bracketing pKa values)
2. Compare pH to pI:
   - pH > pI → net negative (migrates toward anode in electrophoresis)
   - pH < pI → net positive (migrates toward cathode in electrophoresis)
   - pH = pI → net zero (does not migrate)

For electrophoresis migration: positively charged amino acid moves toward the negative electrode (cathode). Negatively charged moves toward positive electrode (anode). Use Henderson-Hasselbalch for the R group specifically: if pH > pKa(R group), that group is deprotonated (base form).

## Common Trap
⚠️ His has pKa ~6 for its R group, meaning it is the ONLY amino acid that significantly changes protonation state near physiological pH (6–8 range). This makes His uniquely suited for enzyme active site acid-base catalysis — it can accept or donate protons at physiological pH. Students often overlook His when asked which amino acid participates in acid-base catalysis at pH 7. Also: disulfide bonds require OXIDIZING conditions (not reducing); reducing agents (DTT, β-mercaptoethanol) BREAK disulfide bonds.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** Aspartic acid has pKa values of 2.1 (α-COOH), 3.9 (R-group COOH), and 9.8 (α-NH₃⁺). What is its pI, and what is its net charge at pH 7?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**pI ≈ 3.0; net charge at pH 7 is approximately −1.** The pI is the average of the two pKa values that flank the neutral (zwitterion) form. For Asp, the neutral form has: COOH groups deprotonated (pKa 2.1 and 3.9) but NH₃⁺ still protonated. pI = (2.1 + 3.9)/2 = 3.0. At pH 7, all three groups are: α-COOH (pKa 2.1) = deprotonated (−1), R-COOH (pKa 3.9) = deprotonated (−1), α-NH₃⁺ (pKa 9.8) = protonated (+1). Net charge = −1 − 1 + 1 = −1.
</details>

**Q2.** An amino acid with pI = 9.5 is placed in a gel electrophoresis buffer at pH 7.0. Which direction does it migrate, and toward which electrode?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**It migrates toward the cathode (negative electrode).** pH (7.0) < pI (9.5) → net positive charge. At pH 7.0, the amino acid has not yet lost enough protons to reach its isoelectric point (which requires pH 9.5). A positively charged molecule is attracted to the negative pole (cathode). This amino acid is likely basic (Lys, Arg, or His).
</details>

**Q3.** A protein is treated with β-mercaptoethanol (a reducing agent). What happens to its tertiary structure?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Disulfide bonds are broken, disrupting tertiary structure.** β-Mercaptoethanol is a reducing agent that cleaves −S−S− bonds by reducing them back to two −SH (thiol) groups. Disulfide bonds between Cys residues stabilize tertiary (and quaternary) protein structure. Breaking them can cause the protein to unfold (denature) if those bonds were critical for maintaining the 3D shape. This is also why hair straightening/perming works — it breaks and reforms disulfide bonds in keratin.
</details>

**Q4.** Proline is found frequently at the ends of α-helices but rarely within them. Why?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Proline's cyclic structure prevents it from forming hydrogen bonds within an α-helix.** In Pro, the R group is covalently bonded to the backbone nitrogen, forming a rigid ring. This eliminates the N−H bond needed for the backbone hydrogen bonding that stabilizes α-helices, and restricts the φ (phi) dihedral angle. Pro creates a "kink" or bend in the polypeptide chain — useful at the ends of helices, loop regions, and turns, but incompatible with regular helix geometry.
</details>

**Q5.** In a chymotrypsin active site, His 57 acts as a general base to deprotonate Ser 195. Why is His specifically suited for this role at physiological pH?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**His has a pKa ~6 for its imidazole R group, placing it uniquely close to physiological pH (7.4).** This means His can exist in both protonated and deprotonated forms at physiological pH — it can accept a proton (act as base) or donate one (act as acid) under normal cellular conditions. No other standard amino acid has an R-group pKa near 7. Asp/Glu (pKa ~4) are almost always deprotonated at pH 7; Lys/Arg (pKa >10) are almost always protonated. His is the versatile acid-base catalyst uniquely positioned at physiological pH.
</details>

# Thermodynamics & Gibbs Free Energy
**AAMC Category:** 5E — Principles of chemical thermodynamics and kinetics | **Section:** Chem/Physics | **Tier:** 1 | [[mcat-chem-physics-hub]]

## Core Concept
Gibbs free energy (ΔG) determines whether a reaction is spontaneous. ΔG = ΔH − TΔS: a reaction is spontaneous when ΔG < 0. At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 and the relationship ΔG° = −RTlnKeq links standard free energy to the equilibrium constant. The MCAT tests sign analysis, the effect of temperature on spontaneity, and how coupled reactions (like ATP hydrolysis) drive unfavorable processes.

## High-Yield Facts
- **ΔG = ΔH − TΔS**; spontaneous when ΔG < 0; non-spontaneous when ΔG > 0
- **ΔG = 0**: reaction is at equilibrium (no net driving force)
- **ΔG° = −RTlnKeq**: standard free energy and equilibrium constant are directly linked
- Keq > 1 → ΔG° < 0 → products favored at equilibrium
- Keq < 1 → ΔG° > 0 → reactants favored at equilibrium
- **ΔH < 0** = exothermic (releases heat); **ΔH > 0** = endothermic (absorbs heat)
- **ΔS > 0** = increase in disorder (gas production, dissolution, more particles)
- **ΔS < 0** = decrease in disorder (condensation, crystallization, fewer particles)
- **Four combinations of ΔH/ΔS signs:**
  - ΔH < 0, ΔS > 0: always spontaneous (all temperatures)
  - ΔH > 0, ΔS < 0: never spontaneous (all temperatures)
  - ΔH < 0, ΔS < 0: spontaneous at low T (enthalpy-driven)
  - ΔH > 0, ΔS > 0: spontaneous at high T (entropy-driven)
- **Hess's Law**: ΔH for a reaction = sum of ΔH for component steps
- **ATP hydrolysis** (ΔG° = −30.5 kJ/mol) couples to drive endergonic reactions by summing ΔG values

## Mnemonic
**"Negative H, Positive S = Always Spontaneous"** (NHPS — "Nice Happy Process Succeeds") — this combination always gives ΔG < 0 regardless of temperature.

## ⚡ Expert Shortcut
*Source: Khan Academy MCAT*
Temperature decides spontaneity when signs are mixed:
- Negative ΔH + Positive ΔS = always spontaneous (ΔG always negative)
- Positive ΔH + Negative ΔS = never spontaneous (ΔG always positive)
- Mixed signs: temperature decides which term wins (TΔS). High T favors the entropy term; low T favors the enthalpy term.
For coupled reactions: add the ΔG° values. If the sum is negative, the coupled process is spontaneous overall — this is how ATP makes unfavorable biosynthesis reactions proceed.

## Common Trap
⚠️ ΔG° (with degree symbol) refers to standard conditions (1 M, 1 atm, 25°C). ΔG (no degree symbol) = actual free energy at real concentrations. A reaction with positive ΔG° can still be spontaneous under cellular conditions if reactant concentrations are very high and product concentrations are very low (far from equilibrium). Never confuse standard and actual conditions.

## ✅ Understanding Checkpoints

**Q1.** A reaction has ΔH = −50 kJ/mol and ΔS = −100 J/mol·K. At 500 K, is the reaction spontaneous?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Not spontaneous at 500 K.** ΔG = ΔH − TΔS = −50,000 J − (500)(−100 J/K) = −50,000 + 50,000 = 0 J/mol. ΔG = 0 exactly at 500 K (equilibrium temperature). Below 500 K, ΔG < 0 (spontaneous); above 500 K, ΔG > 0 (non-spontaneous). The crossover temperature is T = ΔH/ΔS = 50,000/100 = 500 K.
</details>

**Q2.** A reaction has Keq = 1000 at 25°C. What is the sign of ΔG°, and are products or reactants favored?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**ΔG° is negative; products are strongly favored.** Since Keq > 1, ln(Keq) > 0, so ΔG° = −RTlnKeq is negative. Keq = 1000 means products are favored ~1000:1 over reactants at equilibrium. The larger the Keq, the more negative ΔG°.
</details>

**Q3.** ATP hydrolysis has ΔG° = −30.5 kJ/mol. A biosynthetic reaction has ΔG° = +20 kJ/mol. If ATP hydrolysis is coupled to this reaction, what is the net ΔG°?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Net ΔG° = −10.5 kJ/mol (spontaneous).** Add the two ΔG° values: −30.5 + 20 = −10.5 kJ/mol. Because the net is negative, the coupled reaction is thermodynamically favorable. This is how cells use ATP hydrolysis to power endergonic reactions — the energy released by ATP drives the unfavorable step.
</details>

**Q4.** Ice melting (H₂O solid → H₂O liquid) is endothermic (ΔH > 0) and increases entropy (ΔS > 0). Why does ice spontaneously melt at room temperature (25°C) but not at −10°C?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Temperature determines which term dominates.** ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. With ΔH > 0 and ΔS > 0, ΔG is negative only when TΔS > ΔH — i.e., at high enough temperature. At 25°C (298 K), TΔS exceeds ΔH, so ΔG < 0 (melting is spontaneous). At −10°C (263 K), TΔS < ΔH, so ΔG > 0 (melting is non-spontaneous; ice stays frozen). The crossover is at 0°C, where ΔG = 0 (equilibrium between solid and liquid).
</details>

**Q5.** A phase transition from liquid to gas has ΔS > 0. How does increasing pressure affect the spontaneity of vaporization?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**Increasing pressure makes vaporization less spontaneous.** Higher external pressure compresses the gas phase, effectively raising the free energy of the gas relative to the liquid. This shifts equilibrium back toward the liquid phase (Le Chatelier's principle applied thermodynamically). The boiling point rises with pressure — more energy (higher temperature) is needed to make vaporization spontaneous against the higher pressure. This is why pressure cookers raise the boiling point of water above 100°C.
</details>

# MCAT Content and Preparation

## Overview

The [[mcat]] is organized around 10 foundational concepts and 4 scientific inquiry and reasoning skills. Understanding the exam's conceptual architecture is essential for efficient preparation, since the [[aamc]] explicitly maps each question to a foundational concept × reasoning skill intersection.

The full content specification is documented in [[mcat-content-outline-2020]], which is the same document given to item writers who create exam questions.

## The Four Sections

### Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems
Covers processes unique to living organisms: molecular biology, genetics, metabolism, cell biology, organ systems. Heavily biology-weighted (~65% BIO). Key themes: how biomolecules (proteins, nucleic acids, lipids, carbohydrates) determine cell structure and function; how organ systems maintain homeostasis.

Foundational Concepts: FC1 (biomolecules), FC2 (cells and organisms), FC3 (organ systems and homeostasis).

### Chemical and Physical Foundations of Biological Systems
Covers physics and chemistry as they apply to biological systems: mechanics, fluids, electrochemistry, optics, atomic structure, thermodynamics, organic chemistry of biomolecules. Equally weighted between Gen Chem and Physics (~35% each). Key themes: physical principles governing transport, signaling, and chemical reactions in living systems.

Foundational Concepts: FC4 (physical processes in organisms), FC5 (chemical principles in living systems).

### Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations of Behavior
Covers psychology and sociology as they relate to health and clinical medicine. Heavily psychology-weighted (~65% PSY). Key themes: perception, cognition, behavior, social processes, social stratification, health disparities.

Foundational Concepts: FC6 (perception and cognition), FC7 (behavior), FC8 (self and social interaction), FC9 (culture and social structure), FC10 (social stratification).

### Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS)
No content to memorize — tests reading comprehension and inference on unseen passages from humanities and social sciences. The three skills tested (Foundations of Comprehension, Reasoning Within the Text, Reasoning Beyond the Text) assess how well applicants can understand, analyze, and extend written arguments.

## The Four Scientific Inquiry and Reasoning Skills

These cross-cutting skills are assessed on every science section and represent how the [[aamc]] thinks about scientific competency:

1. **Knowledge** — recognize and recall concepts, identify relationships
2. **Reasoning/Problem-Solving** — apply theories, use mathematical models, evaluate explanations
3. **Research Design** — identify variables, evaluate methodology, reason about ethics
4. **Data and Statistics** — interpret graphs/tables, apply statistical reasoning, draw conclusions

Questions are written at the intersection of a foundational concept (content) and a reasoning skill, so mastery of both content knowledge and analytical skills is required.

## Preparation Implications

- The course abbreviations in [[mcat-content-outline-2020]] (BIO, BC, GC, OC, PHY, PSY, SOC) specify the level of depth required per topic — a topic tagged only BIO need not be studied at the biochemistry level
- The periodic table is provided; no calculator is provided
- CARS cannot be "content-prepped" — it requires practice with timed reading and inference under pressure
- The psych/soc section covers sociological theory (Durkheim, Weber, Merton), health disparities, and demographic concepts that are distinct from typical pre-med coursework

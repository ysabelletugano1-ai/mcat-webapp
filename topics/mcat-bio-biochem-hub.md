## Tutor Strategy

1. **"What happens if this protein is absent or mutated?"** — The MCAT loves loss-of-function scenarios. For any enzyme or receptor question, ask this before reading the choices.
2. **Follow the electrons and the protons** — Most Bio/Biochem questions trace energy flow. Know where ATP, NADH, and FADH₂ come from and where they go.
3. **Passage figures are there for a reason** — If a graph or table appears in the passage, at least one question requires interpreting it. Read the axes before the question.
4. **Know your inhibition types cold** — Enzyme inhibition is tested every single exam. A Lineweaver-Burk graph appears more often than not.
5. **Genetics: always draw the cross** — Don't try to solve inheritance problems in your head. 30 seconds drawing a Punnett square saves 2 minutes of confusion.

## Common Traps

- **Confusing DNA pol I and DNA pol III** — Pol III synthesizes; Pol I removes RNA primer. MCAT asks about both.
- **Mixing up MHC I vs. MHC II** — MHC I → CD8+ (cytotoxic); MHC II → CD4+ (helper). The number product rule: 1×1=1, 2×2=4 → MHC I with CD8 (single digit), MHC II with CD4 (double digit... wait, just memorize it as I→8 is wrong; MHC I goes with CD8).
- **Forgetting Krebs runs twice per glucose** — Every molecule of glucose → 2 pyruvate → 2 acetyl-CoA → Krebs ×2. Double all Krebs outputs.
- **Active vs. passive transport** — "Active" = against gradient AND requires ATP. Facilitated diffusion is passive even though it uses a carrier protein.
- **Gluconeogenesis ≠ reverse glycolysis** — Three irreversible steps in glycolysis are bypassed by unique enzymes in gluconeogenesis.
